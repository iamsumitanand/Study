Support Vector Machines

The algorithm that doesn't just find a boundary — it finds the widest one.

When every other algorithm draws the first line that works, SVMs draw the widest one. That single obsession — maximize the gap between classes rather than merely separate them — is what made SVMs the best image classifiers in the world for over a decade, before deep learning took over. The principle it discovered still lives inside modern neural networks.

The Intuition

The widest street analogy

Imagine two neighbourhoods separated by a street. You could draw the street boundary anywhere between them. A narrow street is risky — a single outlier on either side might fall on the wrong side. But if you insist on the widest possible street, you push both neighbourhoods as far from the boundary as they can go. New visitors arriving near the edge have much more room to be classified correctly. That is exactly what an SVM does with data.

The "street" is the margin — the gap between the decision boundary and the nearest training points from each class. SVMs find the hyperplane that maximizes this margin. The training points that sit right on the edge of the margin — the ones that would fall on the street kerb — are the support vectors. They are the only points the decision boundary actually cares about.

  • The decision boundary is the centreline of the street — the hyperplane itself.
  • The margin lines are the two kerbs, one per class, running parallel to the boundary.
  • The support vectors are the training points that touch the kerbs — remove any other point and the boundary would not change.
  • The margin width is the thing being maximized — wider is safer for unseen data.

CS231n frames this as a loss function: the SVM "wants" the correct class score to exceed every other class score by at least a fixed margin Δ. Points that already satisfy this condition contribute zero loss — the algorithm is satisfied and stops caring about them. Only the points near or inside the margin drive the optimization. This is the "support vector" insight: the solution is defined entirely by the boundary cases.

Understanding It Deeply

Three questions that reveal the core ideas

Think first Why does maximizing the margin reduce generalization error? If a classifier already separates the training classes perfectly with a thin boundary, what goes wrong when it sees new data slightly different from training?

A thin margin means the boundary hugged the training data as tightly as possible. Any new point landing near the boundary — slightly shifted due to noise, lighting change, measurement error — is likely to end up on the wrong side. A wider margin acts as a buffer against exactly this kind of perturbation. Statistically, the maximum-margin hyperplane minimizes an upper bound on the generalization error (the VC dimension bound). The margin width is a direct proxy for how robust the classifier is to small shifts in the input distribution.

Think first What happens when the two classes aren't linearly separable at all? If no straight line can separate them in the original space, does the SVM simply fail?

Two answers, one for each situation. First, the soft margin: allow some points to violate the margin, but penalize each violation. The parameter C controls the tradeoff — high C means almost no violations allowed (tight boundary), low C means violations are tolerated (smooth boundary). This handles noisy, overlapping classes in the original feature space.

Second, the kernel trick: map the data into a higher-dimensional space where a linear separator does exist. Crucially, you never compute the transformation explicitly — you only compute dot products in the new space via a kernel function K(xᵢ, xⱼ). An RBF kernel, for instance, implicitly maps each point into infinite-dimensional space. The entire SVM optimization only ever needs these dot products, so the trick is computationally free — no curse of dimensionality.

Think first Why do only the support vectors matter for the final decision? The SVM is trained on all N points, so why can all non-support-vector points be removed after training without changing a single prediction?

Because the decision boundary is defined by the margin constraints — and the margin constraints are only tight (binding) at the support vectors. In the dual formulation of the SVM optimization, each training point has a coefficient αᵢ. For points safely inside the correct side of the margin, the constraint is not tight: αᵢ = 0. These points contribute nothing to the decision function. The decision boundary is literally a weighted sum over support vectors only. This is why SVMs are memory-efficient at prediction time — only support vectors are stored. It also explains why SVMs don't degrade with irrelevant training points far from the boundary.

The Math

Maximum margin objective and hinge loss

The SVM solves a constrained optimization problem. The objective is deceptively simple: minimize the length of the weight vector — which is equivalent to maximizing the margin.

Hard-Margin SVM Objective (linearly separable case)
min ½||w||² subject to: yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b) ≥ 1 for all i
w weight vector — defines the orientation of the hyperplane b bias — shifts the hyperplane away from the origin ||w|| norm of w — inversely proportional to margin width (margin = 2/||w||) yᵢ ∈ {−1, +1} class label for training point i xᵢ feature vector for training point i
Soft-Margin SVM (with slack variables)
min ½||w||² + C·Σ ζᵢ subject to: yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b) ≥ 1 − ζᵢ, ζᵢ ≥ 0
ζᵢ slack variable — how much point i is allowed to violate the margin C penalty parameter — high C: tight margin, few violations; low C: wide margin, more violations allowed
Hinge Loss Formulation (equivalent to soft-margin SVM)
L = (1/N)·Σ max(0, 1 − yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b)) + λ||w||²
max(0, …) hinge loss — zero when margin is satisfied; increases linearly as violation grows λ regularization strength — equivalent to 1/(2CN); balances loss vs. margin 1 − yᵢ(…) the margin violation — negative when point is correctly classified with margin ≥ 1

Four-step worked example — computing margin on a toy dataset:

Dataset: 4 points in 2D. Class +1: A = (1, 2), B = (2, 3). Class −1: C = (−1, −1), D = (0, −2). Candidate hyperplane: w = (1, 1), b = 0 (i.e., x₁ + x₂ = 0).

1 Compute ||w|| ||w|| = √(1² + 1²) = √2 ≈ 1.414
2 Functional margins A: y·(w·x+b) = +1·(1+2+0) = +3
B: +1·(2+3+0) = +5
C: −1·(−1−1+0) = +2
D: −1·(0−2+0) = +2
3 Geometric margin = min(functional margins) / ||w|| = 2 / √2 = √2 ≈ 1.414
4 Margin width = 2 × geometric margin = 2√2 ≈ 2.828. Points C and D are the support vectors — they have the smallest functional margin (2). The SVM would adjust w and b to push this margin wider. The constraint yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b) ≥ 1 is satisfied for all 4 points, so hinge loss = 0 — the margin is already feasible.

From Toy to Real

From two blobs to face recognition

SVMs dominated computer vision for over a decade before deep learning. Understanding why they worked — and exactly where they broke down — explains what deep learning had to improve upon.

In the early 2000s, face recognition systems (AT&T, Viola-Jones) used SVMs on raw pixel features or hand-crafted descriptors (Histogram of Oriented Gradients, SIFT). A 64×64 grayscale face image becomes a 4,096-dimensional vector. The SVM finds a hyperplane in this 4,096-D space that separates "face" from "non-face".

With an RBF kernel, the SVM could capture complex, non-linear boundaries in pixel space — essentially learning which pixel intensity patterns are consistent with a face. On benchmark datasets like LFW (Labeled Faces in the Wild), linear SVMs on HOG features achieved 95%+ accuracy in 2009. No other algorithm came close.

Why SVMs eventually lost to deep learning: SVMs require hand-crafted features (HOG, SIFT) fed to them as input. The kernel trick is powerful, but it operates on whatever features you designed. Deep networks learn their own features end-to-end — they discover that edges matter, then corners, then face parts — without being told. The SVM is the world's best boundary-finder for a fixed feature space. It just can't learn the feature space itself.

Today, SVMs remain the algorithm of choice when:

  • Training data is small — SVMs generalize well even with hundreds of examples, where neural networks overfit.
  • Features > samples — text classification (bag-of-words gives 100,000 features from 1,000 documents) is a natural fit.
  • Interpretability matters — support vectors are explicit training examples, not abstract weight matrices.
  • Speed of prediction — once trained, SVM inference is a single dot product over the support vectors.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs — and how they connect

These aren't five random mistakes. Each one leads naturally to the next — correct the first and the others start to crack.

  • ① SVMs only work for linearly separable data.
    This confuses the hard-margin case (the textbook introduction) with the full algorithm. The kernel trick maps data into a higher-dimensional space where a linear separator exists — an RBF kernel can in principle separate any dataset. Separately, the soft-margin extension allows boundary violations even without a kernel. Real SVMs handle non-linear, overlapping classes routinely.
  • ② The kernel trick means SVMs compute features in high-dimensional space, which is slow.
    The key insight of the kernel trick is that you never compute the high-dimensional representation explicitly. The SVM dual formulation only requires dot products K(xᵢ, xⱼ) — and kernels compute these directly in the original space. An RBF kernel K(x, x') = exp(−γ||x−x'||²) requires the same operations as a euclidean distance, regardless of the implicit feature space dimension.
  • ③ More training data always helps an SVM become more accurate.
    Unlike neural networks, SVMs don't improve proportionally with raw data volume. Training complexity is O(n² to n³) in the number of samples — doubling the dataset can quadruple or octuple training time. SVMs genuinely shine when data is scarce and high-dimensional. With millions of samples, linear models or neural networks are almost always the better choice.
  • ④ All training points contribute to the decision boundary.
    Only the support vectors do — those are the points whose dual coefficient αᵢ > 0. All other training points, regardless of how many there are, have αᵢ = 0 and contribute nothing to the decision function. This is why SVMs are called "sparse" classifiers. After training, only the support vectors need to be retained for prediction. This is also why SVMs are robust to outliers far from the boundary — they're simply ignored.
  • ⑤ Setting C very high guarantees a better model — it finds the most accurate boundary possible.
    High C means the SVM is heavily penalized for any margin violation, forcing it to fit the training data tightly. This leads to a narrow margin and overfitting — a boundary that memorizes noise in the training set and fails on new data. The optimal C is a hyperparameter that must be tuned (typically via cross-validation). Low C with noisy data often generalizes far better than high C, even though training accuracy is lower.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • SVMs find the hyperplane that maximizes the margin — the gap between the decision boundary and the nearest training points from each class. Wider margin = better generalization.
  • Only support vectors (the points on or inside the margin) determine the decision boundary. Remove all other training points after training — nothing changes.
  • Hinge loss: max(0, 1 − yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b)). Zero once the margin is satisfied. Increases linearly with margin violations. Non-differentiable at zero (subgradient methods needed).
  • The soft-margin parameter C trades off margin width against tolerance for violations. High C → narrow margin, few training errors. Low C → wide margin, more training errors but better generalization on noisy data.
  • The kernel trick enables non-linear boundaries by implicitly mapping to higher-dimensional space. Only dot products K(xᵢ, xⱼ) are needed — the transformation is never computed explicitly.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: SVMs produce a single boundary that divides the space into two classes. But what if there are no class labels at all? What if you have a dataset and you want to discover whether there's any structure in it — without being told what to look for?

That's unsupervised learning, and the simplest version of it is K-Means Clustering. It partitions your data into groups based purely on proximity — no labels needed. Which is exactly the next chapter.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 A trained SVM has 8 support vectors out of 1,000 training points. You delete the other 992 points. Do any predictions change? ▼
No — not a single prediction changes. The SVM decision function is a weighted sum over support vectors only: Σᵢ∈SV yᵢαᵢK(xᵢ, x) + b. Points with αᵢ = 0 (all non-support-vectors) contribute nothing to this sum. The 992 deleted points have αᵢ = 0 because their margin constraints were satisfied with slack — they're not on the margin boundary. Only the 8 support vectors define the boundary.
02 You increase C from 0.1 to 100 on a noisy dataset. Training accuracy goes up but test accuracy drops. What did you do wrong? ▼
You overfit by forcing the SVM to memorize noise. High C penalizes margin violations heavily, shrinking the margin to fit every training point — including noisy outliers. The boundary becomes complex and specific to the training set. On a noisy dataset, a lower C gives a wider, smoother boundary that tolerates violations near the decision surface, generalizing better to test data. C must be tuned via cross-validation; higher is not always better.
03 What does the hinge loss max(0, 1 − yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b)) equal for a point that is correctly classified with a margin of 2? ▼
Zero. A correctly classified point with margin 2 satisfies yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b) = 2. Plugging in: max(0, 1 − 2) = max(0, −1) = 0. The hinge loss is zero whenever the margin ≥ 1. This is the "happy zone" — the SVM contributes no gradient from this point. Only points with margin < 1 (inside or across the boundary) drive the optimization. This is what makes SVMs sparse in their loss: satisfied points disappear entirely.
04 An RBF kernel SVM draws a non-linear boundary in 2D. Where does the actual linear boundary exist? ▼
In the implicit infinite-dimensional feature space defined by the kernel. The RBF kernel K(x, x') = exp(−γ||x − x'||²) implicitly corresponds to a dot product φ(x)·φ(x') in an infinite-dimensional space. The SVM finds a linear hyperplane in that space — which projects back as a curved boundary in the original 2D space. Crucially, the transformation φ is never computed. Only K(xᵢ, xⱼ) values are needed. The "non-linear boundary" is an artifact of the projection back to input space.
05 Two SVMs are trained on the same data. SVM-A has 300 support vectors; SVM-B has 12. Which is likely to generalize better, and why? ▼
SVM-B (fewer support vectors) likely generalizes better. A large number of support vectors means many training points are on or inside the margin — the boundary is complex, hugging the training data tightly. This typically indicates a small margin (high C or a poorly tuned kernel), which is prone to overfitting. Fewer support vectors means a wider, simpler boundary backed by less training evidence but more resistant to noise. The ratio of support vectors to training size is an informal proxy for model complexity.
06 You have 10 features and 50,000 training examples. A colleague suggests using an RBF SVM. What concern should you raise? ▼
Training complexity — SVM scales as O(n² to n³) in sample count. With 50,000 samples, the kernel matrix is 50,000 × 50,000 — potentially 20GB of memory just to store it. Training may take hours to days. For this regime (many samples, few features), LinearSVC or logistic regression trained with stochastic gradient descent would be far faster and often similarly accurate. RBF SVMs are powerful but are fundamentally designed for the small-sample, high-feature regime, not large datasets.
07 The margin width for a hard-margin SVM is 2/||w||. So why does the SVM minimize ½||w||² rather than directly maximizing 2/||w||? ▼
Mathematical convenience — minimizing ½||w||² is equivalent but easier to optimize. Maximizing 2/||w|| is a non-convex fractional objective. Minimizing ½||w||² (equivalently minimizing ||w||²) is convex and quadratic. The ½ factor makes the gradient exactly w (removing a factor of 2 from differentiation). The resulting optimization is a convex quadratic program — guaranteed to find a unique global minimum efficiently using standard solvers. The two problems have the same solution; the reformulation is purely for numerical tractability.
08 Why must you scale your features before training an SVM? ▼
SVMs are not scale-invariant — large-scale features dominate the margin calculation. The margin width is 2/||w||, and ||w|| is computed in feature space. A feature with range [0, 10,000] contributes gradient components 10,000× larger than a feature with range [0, 1]. The SVM will align its hyperplane primarily along the large-scale direction — even if that feature is less informative. Standardizing to mean=0, variance=1 (or scaling to [−1, +1]) ensures all features contribute proportionally. This is not optional for SVMs — scikit-learn's docs call it "critical."
09 CS231n notes that the SVM "is happy once the margins are satisfied." A Softmax classifier on the same data keeps adjusting weights even after all points are correctly classified. Which behavior is more robust in practice? ▼
It depends on the task — both behaviors have distinct advantages. The SVM's "satisfaction" means it ignores points already inside the correct margin — it's sparse and focused only on boundary cases. This makes it robust to easy points far from the boundary and computationally efficient once margins are satisfied. Softmax keeps pushing all probabilities toward their targets, which can improve calibrated probability estimates and often yields better performance when probabilities matter. For hard classification without probability estimates, SVM behavior is generally more robust; for probability-calibrated outputs, Softmax is preferred.
