Part 4 · Generative AI

Large Language Models

How predicting the next word taught machines to reason, write, and think.

← Diffusion Models Fine-tuning & RLHF →
A language model trained on text doesn't learn words — it learns a compressed model of how humans think and express ideas. The objective is trivial: predict the next token. What emerges is not.

The Intuition

Next-token prediction, trained on all of human writing

A language model is a function trained on a single self-supervised task: given all the words so far, predict what word comes next. No labels required — every sentence in every document on the internet is its own training example. The input is the context; the label is the very next token. Run this on hundreds of billions of words and something unexpected happens: the model doesn't just learn grammar. It learns to reason.

This is the central mystery of modern AI. No one explicitly taught GPT-4 to write code, explain concepts, or solve logic puzzles. These are emergent capabilities — abilities that appear at scale that were not present at smaller sizes. A model trained on 10 billion tokens can't do arithmetic. Train the same architecture on 500 billion tokens with 100 billion parameters and it can. The capability wasn't programmed; it fell out of learning to predict text extremely well.

  • The token is the unit — roughly a word or word-piece. "unbelievable" → ["un", "believ", "able"].
  • The context window is how many past tokens the model can see — GPT-4 sees ~128k tokens at once.
  • The training objective is cross-entropy loss over every position — minimize surprise at each next token.
  • The emergent behavior is why scale matters — capabilities appear discontinuously as size and data increase.

Understanding It Deeply

Three questions that go to the heart of it

Next-token prediction looks trivial on the surface. But if you think carefully about what it requires — and what surprises it has produced — three deep questions emerge. Work through them before reading the answers.

Think first Why does predicting the next token teach reasoning, math, and coding — not just grammar? The training signal is nothing more than "what word comes next." How can that teach a model to prove theorems?

Because to predict the next token well across all of the internet, you must internalize the world that generated the text. A model that sees "The proof proceeds by induction. Base case: n=1..." and must predict the next line has to understand what a valid induction proof looks like — not just the surface grammar. The same applies to code, logic, and science. You can't fake your way to low cross-entropy on millions of diverse technical texts. You have to learn the underlying structure. Predicting text is a proxy for modeling the minds that wrote it.

Think first What does "in-context learning" mean, and why is it surprising? When you give GPT-4 a few examples in the prompt and it generalizes to new cases — what is actually happening? No weights are updated. No gradient steps are taken. How?

In-context learning is the model's ability to use patterns in its context window as implicit training signal at inference time — without any weight updates. This was not designed in. It emerged. Researchers believe the transformer's attention mechanism learned, during pretraining, to implement a form of gradient descent internally — recognizing patterns in the prompt and adjusting its predictions accordingly. The model isn't memorizing your examples; it's using its billion-parameter generalization engine to extrapolate from them. This is why few-shot prompting works even on tasks the model has never explicitly seen.

Think first What is the difference between a base model and an instruction-tuned model? If GPT-4 and its base model have the same weights at the start of fine-tuning, what does fine-tuning change — and what doesn't it change?

A base model is a pure next-token predictor — it will complete "How do I bake a cake?" by continuing the text as if it were a recipe book, not by answering you. Fine-tuning with human feedback (RLHF) teaches it to play the role of a helpful assistant. Crucially, fine-tuning doesn't add knowledge — all the world knowledge is already baked into the base model's weights from pretraining. Fine-tuning shapes the behavior: the model learns to respond in a particular format, to follow instructions, to refuse harmful requests, and to rank helpful answers higher. You're not filling an empty vessel — you're steering a very full one.

The Math

Autoregressive generation and softmax temperature

An LLM generates text one token at a time. Each token's probability depends on all the tokens that came before. Two equations govern this completely: the autoregressive factorization and the temperature-scaled softmax.

Autoregressive Factorization
p(x) = ∏t=1T p(xt | x1, …, xt−1)
x the full sequence of tokens — a sentence, paragraph, or document xt the token at position t — predicted one at a time, left to right ∏ product — the joint probability of the whole sequence is the product of each step's probability p(xt | …)the model's next-token distribution — a softmax over the vocabulary at each step
Softmax with Temperature
p(xt = k) = exp(zk / τ) / Σj exp(zj / τ)
zk logit for token k — the raw score from the model's final linear layer before softmax τ temperature — scales all logits before softmax; τ < 1 sharpens, τ > 1 flattens exp(·) exponential — ensures all probabilities are positive; larger logits get exponentially more mass Σ exp the partition function — normalizes so all probabilities sum to 1

Four steps — computing next-token probabilities from logits:

TokenLogit zz / τ (τ=1)exp(z/τ)Probability
1 mat 2.10 2.10 8.166 0.41
2 floor 1.20 1.20 3.320 0.18
3 sofa 0.85 0.85 2.340 0.12
4 roof 0.40 0.40 1.492 0.09
Σ — — — ~19.78 1.00
Now apply temperature τ = 0.5 (greedy-ish). Divide all logits by 0.5 before softmax: 2.10→4.20, 1.20→2.40, etc. The top token "mat" now gets exp(4.20) / Σ ≈ 0.74 — much more dominant. Temperature is division before exponentiation, so its effect is exponentially amplified. Halving τ doesn't double the top probability; it raises it to a much higher power.

Real Models

GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — and why size and data both matter

Every major LLM — GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — is a transformer trained on next-token prediction at enormous scale. The architecture is nearly identical across all of them. What varies is scale, data quality, and the fine-tuning recipe.

Architecture overview. All frontier LLMs are decoder-only transformers. Input tokens are embedded into vectors, passed through dozens or hundreds of transformer blocks (each containing self-attention + feed-forward layers), and projected back to a vocabulary distribution. GPT-4 is estimated at ~1.8 trillion parameters across a mixture-of-experts architecture. Claude 3 and Gemini 1.5 are similar in scale and share the same basic design. What differs is training data curation, context length (Gemini 1.5 handles 1M tokens), and the fine-tuning objectives.

Scaling laws (Chinchilla). For years, the field believed bigger models were always better. The 2022 Chinchilla paper (Hoffmann et al., DeepMind) overturned this. It showed that for a given compute budget, you should train a smaller model on more data rather than a larger model on less data. The optimal ratio is approximately 20 tokens of training data per model parameter. GPT-3 (175B parameters) was significantly undertrained — it would have been more capable if trained on 3.5 trillion tokens rather than 300 billion. The Chinchilla insight reshaped how every major lab trains its models.

Why both size and data matter — and why you can't trade one for the other: Model capacity (parameters) determines what patterns the model can represent. Training data determines what patterns it's exposed to. A small model on vast data hits a capacity ceiling — it can't store what it sees. A large model on thin data memorizes rather than generalizes. Chinchilla showed the optimal frontier: roughly 20 tokens per parameter. Below this line, data is the bottleneck. Above it, capacity is. The major labs now all train on trillions of tokens across hundreds of billions of parameters — because that's where the frontier is.

Common Misconceptions

Five causal misconceptions about LLMs

These misconceptions form a chain. Each one fails to account for what actually happens at scale. Trace the chain and none of them can survive contact with the evidence.

  • ① LLMs understand language the way humans do.
    LLMs model statistical distributions over token sequences. They have no grounding in physical reality, no sensory experience, no persistent memory across conversations. "Understanding" as humans experience it — tied to embodied action and consequence — is not what's happening. What they do have is an extraordinary capacity to represent and manipulate the latent structure of language. That is genuinely impressive and genuinely different from human cognition.
  • ② Therefore LLMs are just stochastic parrots — they only repeat patterns from training data.
    This overcorrects. The stochastic parrot framing predicts that LLMs shouldn't generalize to novel inputs outside their training distribution. But they do — they solve novel math problems, write code for APIs that didn't exist during training, and reason about counterfactuals. The compression required to achieve low loss on trillions of diverse tokens cannot be achieved by simple pattern matching. Something structural is being learned. The debate is about the nature of that structure, not whether generalization occurs.
  • ③ Then size is all that matters — bigger is always better.
    The Chinchilla result directly refutes this. For a fixed compute budget, an undertrained large model is consistently outperformed by an optimally trained smaller model. GPT-3 (175B) was outperformed on many benchmarks by Chinchilla (70B trained on 4× more data). Size and data interact — the optimal frontier is roughly 20 tokens of training per parameter. More parameters with insufficient data leads to memorization, not generalization.
  • ④ LLMs memorize their training data rather than generalizing from it.
    Memorization and generalization coexist. LLMs can recite passages verbatim from training data (especially frequently-repeated text) — this is a real privacy and copyright concern. But the same model also generalizes to tasks and combinations it has never seen. A neural network with 100B parameters trained on 2T tokens cannot memorize everything — it must compress. The weights represent a generalized model of language, not a lookup table. Both phenomena are real; neither fully characterizes what LLMs do.
  • ⑤ Hallucinations are bugs that will be patched away in future versions.
    Hallucinations are a structural consequence of autoregressive generation, not a software bug. The model always produces the highest-probability continuation given its context — it has no mechanism to distinguish "things I know" from "things I'm confabulating." When the model generates a plausible-sounding but false fact, it's doing exactly what it was trained to do: produce fluent, contextually appropriate text. Reducing hallucinations requires either grounding (RAG), explicit uncertainty modeling, or training on human feedback that rewards saying "I don't know" — none of which fully eliminate the problem.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • LLMs are decoder-only transformers trained on next-token prediction via cross-entropy loss. The training signal is self-supervised — no manual labels required.
  • Emergent capabilities (reasoning, coding, math) were not designed in — they arise at scale as a byproduct of extremely good next-token prediction across diverse text.
  • Generation is autoregressive: p(x) = ∏ p(x_t | x_{1..t-1}). Temperature τ scales logits before softmax — lower τ sharpens, higher τ flattens the distribution.
  • Chinchilla scaling laws: optimal training uses ~20 tokens per parameter. A smaller model on more data beats a larger model on less data for the same compute budget.
  • Fine-tuning (RLHF) shapes behavior, not knowledge. Hallucinations are a structural consequence of autoregressive generation, not a patchable bug.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: A base language model will complete "What is the capital of France?" by generating more text that looks like it came from a training document — perhaps an essay, a list, or a continuation of whatever context it was given. It won't answer you. So how do you take a model that predicts text and turn it into a model that follows instructions, refuses harmful requests, and sounds like a helpful assistant?

That's fine-tuning and RLHF. And it's the next chapter.

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 An LLM is trained purely on next-token prediction. No task labels, no explicit instructions. Yet GPT-4 can write Python, solve calculus problems, and explain quantum mechanics. How? ▼
Emergent capabilities from scale. To predict the next token with low cross-entropy loss across hundreds of billions of words of Python documentation, math papers, and physics textbooks, the model must internalize the underlying structure of those domains. The training signal isn't "learn to code" — it's "predict text perfectly." But doing that perfectly across diverse technical domains requires learning the things that generated the text. Capabilities appear discontinuously as model size and training data scale up.
02 You have a compute budget of 6×10²³ FLOPs. You can train a 70B parameter model or a 7B parameter model. The Chinchilla paper tells you which is better — what does it say, and why? ▼
It depends on how many tokens each gets. Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022) showed the optimal ratio is ~20 tokens per parameter. A 70B model needs ~1.4 trillion tokens to be compute-optimal. A 7B model needs ~140 billion. If your budget lets the 7B model train on 10× more data, the 7B model wins. If both get the same token count, the 70B model wins on capacity. GPT-3 (175B on 300B tokens) was massively undertrained — Chinchilla 70B on 1.4T tokens outperformed it.
03 Temperature τ = 0.1 vs τ = 2.0. You need to generate 100 different creative story openings. Which do you use and why? ▼
τ = 2.0 (or at least τ > 1.0). At τ = 0.1, the distribution is extremely peaked — the model almost always picks the single highest-probability token, producing near-identical outputs across runs. At τ = 2.0, probability is spread broadly across many tokens, so each generation samples a different path through the distribution. You get diversity at the cost of coherence. For creative variation across 100 runs, you want a flat distribution. For factual Q&A where there's one right answer, you want a sharp one.
04 You give GPT-4 three examples of a new task format in the prompt and it immediately does the task correctly on new inputs — without any fine-tuning. What is happening, and why is this surprising from a learning theory perspective? ▼
In-context learning — a form of task adaptation without weight updates. Classical learning theory says learning requires updating parameters based on gradient signal. Here, no weights change. The model uses the examples in the prompt as implicit signal, and its attention mechanism allows it to "attend" to those examples when predicting outputs. Researchers hypothesize the transformer learned to implement a form of meta-learning during pretraining — its internal computations effectively perform gradient-descent-like adaptation based on in-context examples. It's surprising because no optimization step occurs at inference time.
05 A model generates a plausible-sounding citation to a paper that does not exist. This happens consistently. Is this a bug that will be fixed in the next version? ▼
No — it's a structural property of autoregressive generation. The model does not have a "knowledge" module separate from its generation module. It produces the highest-probability next token given context. A plausible citation looks like a plausible citation — same format, similar author names, a reasonable-sounding title. The model has no way to look up whether that paper exists before generating it. Reducing hallucinations requires retrieval augmentation (RAG), training on explicit uncertainty signals ("I don't know"), or post-hoc verification — not patching the generation mechanism.
06 Logits for a 3-token vocabulary: [3.0, 1.0, 0.0]. Compute the probabilities at τ = 1.0 and at τ = 0.5. What happens to the top token's probability when you halve the temperature? ▼
τ=1.0: exp([3,1,0]) = [20.09, 2.72, 1.00]. Sum = 23.81. p = [0.844, 0.114, 0.042].
τ=0.5: exp([6,2,0]) = [403.4, 7.39, 1.00]. Sum = 411.8. p = [0.980, 0.018, 0.002].
The top token goes from 84% to 98%. Halving temperature doubled the logits before exponentiation — and exponentiation is nonlinear, so doubling the input more than doubles the output. The effect on probability is superlinear: small temperature changes have large effects on how peaked the distribution is.
07 What is the difference between fine-tuning and pretraining in terms of what changes in the model? ▼
Both update weights — but they update what the model "knows" vs. how it "behaves." Pretraining on trillions of tokens fills the model's weights with world knowledge: facts, reasoning patterns, language structure, code syntax. Fine-tuning on thousands of human-preference examples adjusts the same weights to favor certain output styles, instruction-following formats, and refusal behaviors. Fine-tuning doesn't add new knowledge — it shapes which of the model's existing knowledge it surfaces and how. A fine-tuned model that "knows" a fact but was fine-tuned to be evasive will still know the fact; it just won't say it.
08 GPT-3 has 175B parameters and was trained on 300B tokens. Chinchilla has 70B parameters and was trained on 1.4T tokens. They used roughly the same compute. Which performs better, and why? ▼
Chinchilla outperforms GPT-3 on most benchmarks despite being 2.5× smaller. GPT-3 was undertrained — 300B tokens gives roughly 1.7 tokens per parameter, far below the Chinchilla-optimal ~20. The model had high capacity but insufficient exposure to learn how to use it. Chinchilla at 70B × 20 tokens/param = 1.4T tokens is at the compute-optimal frontier. The Chinchilla paper showed this holds across a wide range of model sizes: for any compute budget, the optimal strategy is to train a smaller model on more data, not a larger model on less.
09 A colleague says: "LLMs are just autocomplete — they can't actually reason, they just predict statistically likely tokens." How do you respond? ▼
The description is technically accurate but the conclusion doesn't follow. LLMs do predict statistically likely tokens. But "statistically likely given the context" on problems that require multi-step reasoning means the model must arrive at intermediate correct conclusions to make the final token likely. On chain-of-thought math problems, a wrong intermediate step leads to wrong downstream tokens — so producing correct final answers requires producing correct reasoning chains. Whether this constitutes "real" reasoning is a philosophical question; that it produces correct solutions to novel logic and math problems that simple pattern matching cannot is an empirical fact.
