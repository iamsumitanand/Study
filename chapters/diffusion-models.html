Diffusion Models

How DALL-E and Stable Diffusion turn pure noise into photorealistic images — one denoising step at a time.

DALL-E 3 and Stable Diffusion both start with pure noise and ask a single question: what direction makes this look slightly less like noise? They answer that question, take one tiny step, then ask again. One thousand times. What comes out the other end is a photorealistic image of a concept that never existed.

The Intuition

Destroying information — then learning to reverse it

Diffusion models are built on a two-part idea. The forward process takes a clean image and gradually destroys it by adding a tiny amount of Gaussian noise at each step. After enough steps (typically T = 1000), the image is indistinguishable from pure random noise. This process has no parameters — it's a fixed, mathematical procedure. The reverse process is where learning happens: a neural network trains to predict and remove the noise added at each step, walking the image back toward clean from noise.

Why is this more stable than GANs? In a GAN, a generator and discriminator compete — and that competition is notoriously difficult to balance. The discriminator can overpower the generator, causing mode collapse, or training can oscillate endlessly. Diffusion models sidestep this entirely: there is no adversary. The training objective is simply to predict noise, which is a well-posed regression problem. The loss surface is smooth, gradients are stable, and training converges reliably at scale. The tradeoff is speed — generating a sample requires running the denoising network hundreds of times, versus a single forward pass in a GAN.

  • The forward process is fixed and has no learnable parameters — pure math, T steps of noise addition.
  • The reverse process is learned — a neural network (usually U-Net) predicts the noise at each step.
  • The training signal is trivial to produce: take a clean image, corrupt it, ask the network to predict the corruption.
  • The stability advantage over GANs: the objective is a standard L2 regression — no minimax game.

Understanding It Deeply

Build the intuition before you trust the math

Three questions drive the deepest understanding of diffusion models. Each one reveals a non-obvious design choice that separates diffusion from every prior generative model.

Think first Why does learning to predict the added noise ε work better than learning to predict the clean image x₀ directly? Both are regression tasks. What makes noise prediction the better target?

When the network tries to predict the clean image directly, it must output a complex, high-dimensional distribution that varies wildly depending on image content. At high noise levels (early in denoising), the task is especially hard: almost all information about x₀ is buried, so the prediction is a heavily uncertain mean across all possible clean images — producing blur. Predicting the noise ε is different: noise is i.i.d. Gaussian by construction, with fixed statistics at every timestep. The network always predicts something drawn from the same standard normal distribution, regardless of image content. This consistent target means gradients are stable across all noise levels, and the loss landscape is well-conditioned throughout training. Ho et al. (DDPM, 2020) empirically confirmed this "noise prediction" (also called "ε-prediction") consistently outperforms direct x₀ prediction.

Think first What makes diffusion models more stable than GANs during training, despite being far slower at inference? Name at least one concrete failure mode of GANs that diffusion eliminates by design.

GANs suffer from mode collapse — the generator finds a small set of outputs that fool the discriminator and stops exploring the full data distribution. They also suffer from training instability: because the generator and discriminator co-evolve, the gradients are non-stationary. The discriminator's signal can become uninformative (saturated) or oscillate. Diffusion models have neither problem. Training is a fixed regression against a consistent Gaussian target at every step. There is no adversary, no co-evolution, and no mode collapse — the network learns a probability path from noise to data, and every image in the training set contributes equally via every noise level. The cost is that sampling requires running the denoising network T times (versus once for GANs), making diffusion models significantly slower at inference.

Think first What role does the U-Net architecture specifically play in the reverse process? Why not use a simple fully connected network or a plain convolutional network?

The reverse process requires the network to take a noisy image x_t and a timestep t and output the noise ε that was added at that step. This is fundamentally an image-to-image task — the output must have the same spatial dimensions as the input. U-Net addresses this with its encoder-decoder structure: the encoder compresses the noisy image into a lower-resolution representation (capturing global structure), while the decoder upsamples back to full resolution (recovering spatial detail). The skip connections between encoder and decoder are critical — they let high-frequency detail preserved in the encoder flow directly into the decoder, preventing blurring. The U-Net also accepts the timestep t as a conditioning signal (via sinusoidal embeddings added to each layer), so the network knows how noisy the input is and calibrates its denoising strength accordingly.

The Math

Forward process, reparameterization, and the training objective

The elegance of diffusion models is that the entire forward process can be collapsed to a single closed-form equation — you don't need to run T steps to get x_t from x_0. That makes training efficient.

Forward Process (single step)
q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = N(x_t ; √(1−β_t) · x_{t-1} , β_t · I)
x_t noisy image at timestep t β_t noise schedule — small positive number (e.g. 0.0001 to 0.02), controls how much noise is added √(1−β_t) scaling factor applied to the previous image — shrinks toward zero as noise accumulates N(μ, σ²·I) Gaussian distribution — sample from it to get x_t given x_{t-1}
Reparameterization — closed-form x_t from x_0
x_t = √(ᾱ_t) · x_0 + √(1 − ᾱ_t) · ε where ε ∼ N(0, I)
ᾱ_t cumulative product: ᾱ_t = ∏_{s=1}^{t} (1−β_s) — the retained signal fraction at step t √(ᾱ_t) · x_0 signal component — the original image, scaled down toward zero as t increases √(1−ᾱ_t) · ε noise component — pure Gaussian noise, scaled up as t increases

This reparameterization is key: at any timestep t, we can compute the noisy image directly from x_0 and a single noise sample ε, without simulating t sequential steps. This makes training efficient — just sample a random t, draw ε, compute x_t in one shot, then train the network to predict ε back.

Simplified Training Objective (Ho et al., DDPM 2020)
L = E_{x_0, ε, t} [ ‖ ε − ε_θ(x_t, t) ‖² ]
ε the actual noise added — ground truth, sampled from N(0, I) ε_θ(x_t, t) the noise predicted by the neural network (U-Net) given noisy image x_t and timestep t ‖·‖² mean squared error — just like any regression loss. No adversarial component. E[...] expectation over random x_0 (training images), random ε, and random t

Worked example: compute x_t at t=3 for a 4×4 image

Suppose we have a simple 4×4 grayscale image x_0 (pixel values in [0, 1]). Use a linear noise schedule: β_1=0.1, β_2=0.1, β_3=0.1 (all equal for simplicity).

1 α_t = 1 − β_t = 0.9 Same for all three steps here.
2 ᾱ_3 = α_1 · α_2 · α_3 = 0.9³ = 0.729 Cumulative product of all αs up to t=3.
3 x_3 = √0.729 · x_0 + √(1−0.729) · ε = 0.854 · x_0 + 0.521 · ε ε is drawn once from N(0, I) — same shape as x_0 (4×4).
4 For a pixel with value x_0 = 0.8 and ε = 1.2:  x_3 = 0.854 × 0.8 + 0.521 × 1.2 = 0.683 + 0.625 = 1.308 (clipped to [0,1] for display — noise can push beyond range)
Pattern: after T=1000 steps with typical β schedule, ᾱ_T ≈ 0 and x_T ≈ pure noise. The closed-form formula means training never simulates T sequential steps. It samples t uniformly, computes x_t in one shot, and trains the network to recover ε. Efficient and elegant.

From Theory to Production

DALL-E 3, Stable Diffusion, and Midjourney

Diffusion models didn't just beat GANs on benchmarks — they transformed the entire image generation industry between 2021 and 2023. Here's how the three dominant systems differ and what they share.

Stable Diffusion introduced latent diffusion: instead of running the diffusion process on full-resolution pixels (expensive), it first encodes the image into a compressed latent space using a VAE (variational autoencoder), runs diffusion there, then decodes back to pixels. This made high-resolution generation 4–8× faster and democratized the technology — Stable Diffusion runs on a consumer GPU. The U-Net operates in latent space, and text conditioning is injected via cross-attention with CLIP text embeddings.

DALL-E 3 (OpenAI, 2023) combined a diffusion backbone with a dramatically better text-to-image alignment approach. The key insight: the quality of the text prompt at training time matters enormously. DALL-E 3 used GPT-4 to generate detailed, accurate captions for training images — replacing the short, human-written alt-text that previous models trained on. The result was vastly better prompt following, especially for compositional prompts with multiple objects and relationships.

Midjourney is less technically transparent but is believed to use diffusion with heavy aesthetic fine-tuning. Its distinctive look comes from RLHF-style training on human preference feedback — the model was steered to produce images that humans rate as visually compelling, not just technically accurate.

Text conditioning mechanism: all three systems inject text information into the reverse process via cross-attention. At each denoising step, the U-Net's intermediate features attend over the text embedding — each spatial position in the noisy image "looks at" the text tokens and decides how to denoise based on the meaning of the prompt. This is what allows "a cat wearing a tuxedo at a jazz bar" to produce coherent, prompt-faithful images.

Why diffusion beat GANs for images: GANs won on speed and sharpness but failed on diversity and stability. Training large GANs reliably was an art form — mode collapse, training instability, and poor coverage of rare images were persistent problems. Diffusion models traded speed for coverage and stability. With enough compute and latent compression tricks, the speed gap narrowed enough that the quality and reliability advantages became decisive. By 2022, no new frontier image generation system was GAN-based.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs about diffusion models

These aren't random mistakes. Each one reflects a reasonable guess that happens to be wrong in a revealing way.

  • ① The model is directly learning to "draw" or "paint" an image.
    The model never learns to draw. It learns one narrow skill: predict the noise vector ε that was added to a clean image to produce a noisy image x_t, given x_t and the timestep t. That's a regression task. The "drawing" emerges from applying this denoising step 1000 times starting from pure noise — each step removes a little noise, and the residual structure accumulates into a coherent image. The model has no explicit concept of objects, composition, or style — all of that emerges implicitly from learning the data distribution.
  • ② The reverse process requires running T sequential steps through the full network.
    The training forward process is collapsed to a single step using the closed-form reparameterization (x_t = √ᾱ_t · x_0 + √(1−ᾱ_t) · ε). However, at inference time, the reverse process does require roughly T sequential network evaluations — which is why diffusion is slow to sample. Acceleration methods like DDIM (Song et al., 2020) and DPM-Solver can reduce this to 20–50 steps by using higher-order ODE solvers, trading a small quality reduction for a 10–50× speedup.
  • ③ Text-to-image generation works by training on (image, text) pairs and learning a mapping.
    The diffusion model itself generates images unconditionally — it models p(x). Text conditioning is added via classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021): during training, the text condition is randomly dropped ~10% of the time, training the model to also work unconditionally. At inference, the model runs twice per step — once with the text prompt, once without — and the denoising direction is extrapolated away from the unconditional direction toward the text-conditioned direction. This amplifies prompt adherence at the cost of diversity. The guidance scale controls this tradeoff.
  • ④ Stable Diffusion does diffusion in pixel space, just like DDPM.
    Stable Diffusion uses latent diffusion: it first encodes the image into a compressed latent representation via a VAE (4× or 8× spatial compression), runs the full diffusion process in this latent space, then decodes back to pixels. The U-Net never sees raw pixels during diffusion — only latent codes. This reduces computation by 16–64× and is why Stable Diffusion can run on consumer hardware. DALL-E 3 and other high-end models typically use pixel-space diffusion at multiple resolutions (cascaded diffusion) or hybrid approaches.
  • ⑤ Once fully denoised, running more reverse steps improves quality.
    The reverse process is designed for exactly T steps — it starts at pure noise and reaches the data distribution at step 0. Running additional steps beyond that has no defined meaning: the model was not trained to "further improve" a clean image, only to denoise a noisy one. In practice, "over-denoising" typically degrades the image or introduces artifacts. Post-generation quality improvements (upscaling, sharpening) are done with separate models, not by continuing the reverse diffusion chain.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • Two processes: the forward process adds Gaussian noise step-by-step (fixed, no parameters); the reverse process learns to undo it (U-Net, trained by regression).
  • Closed-form reparameterization: x_t = √ᾱ_t · x_0 + √(1−ᾱ_t) · ε — any noise level can be computed directly from x_0, making training efficient.
  • Training objective is L2 noise prediction: E[‖ε − ε_θ(x_t, t)‖²] — plain regression, no adversarial component, hence stable training.
  • Text conditioning via classifier-free guidance: the model runs twice per step, and the output is guided away from unconditional toward text-conditioned denoising.
  • Diffusion beat GANs on coverage, diversity, and training stability — at the cost of slow inference. Latent diffusion (Stable Diffusion) and DDIM sampling close much of that speed gap.
One question this chapter leaves open: Diffusion models work beautifully for images — continuous data where noise addition is well-defined. But text is discrete. You can't add Gaussian noise to the word "cat." So how do large language models generate coherent sentences? They use a completely different generative paradigm — not denoising, but autoregressive prediction: one token at a time, each conditioned on everything before it.

That's what the next chapter covers.

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 In the forward process, what happens to the signal fraction ᾱ_t as t increases from 0 to T? ▼
ᾱ_t decreases from 1 toward 0. ᾱ_t = ∏_{s=1}^{t} (1−β_s). Since each β_s is a small positive number, each factor (1−β_s) is slightly less than 1. Multiplying T such factors together drives ᾱ_t to near-zero. At t=0, no noise has been added (ᾱ_0 = 1, x_0 = clean image). At t=T, ᾱ_T ≈ 0 and x_T ≈ pure Gaussian noise. The reparameterization x_t = √ᾱ_t · x_0 + √(1−ᾱ_t) · ε shows this directly: as ᾱ_t→0, the signal term vanishes and only noise remains.
02 Why is the simplified training objective L = E[‖ε − ε_θ(x_t, t)‖²] better than training the network to predict x_0 directly? ▼
Noise ε has consistent, fixed statistics (N(0,I)) at every timestep, making it a stable regression target. Predicting x_0 at high noise levels is extremely hard: nearly all information about the clean image is buried, so the best prediction is a blurry average across all plausible images. This creates poorly conditioned gradients. Noise ε is always drawn from the same standard normal, regardless of image content. The network always predicts something from the same distribution, gradients remain stable across all t, and Ho et al. (DDPM) empirically confirmed ε-prediction outperforms direct x_0 prediction.
03 What is the purpose of skip connections in the U-Net used in the reverse process? ▼
Skip connections preserve high-frequency spatial detail that would otherwise be lost during the encoder's downsampling. The U-Net encoder compresses the noisy image into lower resolutions (capturing global structure and semantics). The decoder upsamples back to full resolution. Without skip connections, the decoder must reconstruct fine spatial detail from the compressed bottleneck — producing blurry outputs. Skip connections pipe high-resolution feature maps from the encoder directly into the decoder at matching resolutions, allowing the model to recover sharp, spatially precise details in the denoised output.
04 Stable Diffusion generates 512×512 images but runs diffusion on a 64×64 latent space. What component converts between them, and why does this matter? ▼
A VAE (Variational Autoencoder) — encoder compresses 512×512 pixels → 64×64 latents; decoder expands back. This matters because diffusion in pixel space on a 512×512 image requires the U-Net to process images with 512²=262,144 values per step, hundreds of times. In the 64×64 latent space, each step processes only 64²=4,096 values — a 64× reduction in spatial size. Since each reverse step calls the U-Net, this translates directly into a 64× reduction in compute per step. This is what makes high-resolution generation feasible on consumer GPUs. The VAE is pre-trained separately and kept frozen during diffusion training.
05 Explain classifier-free guidance in one sentence. What tradeoff does the guidance scale control? ▼
Classifier-free guidance runs the denoising network twice per step — once with the text prompt, once without — and amplifies the difference to steer generation toward the prompt. Formally, the guided output = unconditional output + scale × (conditional − unconditional). The guidance scale controls the tradeoff between prompt adherence and output diversity. Low scale (≈1): outputs are diverse but may ignore parts of the prompt. High scale (≈7-15): outputs follow the prompt tightly but are less varied and can look oversaturated or distorted. Most users find 7–9 optimal for general prompts.
06 A GAN and a diffusion model are both trained on the same 100k image dataset. The GAN trains faster and achieves higher FID. Why might you still prefer the diffusion model for a production application? ▼
Diffusion models offer better coverage of the data distribution and more predictable training. GANs with good FID scores may still have mode collapse — they cover the most common images well but fail to generate rare, unusual, or compositionally complex images. In production, you want a model that reliably generates diverse, high-quality outputs across the full prompt distribution, not just on FID benchmark cases. Diffusion models also scale more predictably with data and compute — they don't require careful GAN-specific tricks (progressive growing, spectral norm, careful discriminator tuning) to train. For text-to-image specifically, the cross-attention conditioning mechanism works naturally in diffusion; conditioning GANs on open-ended text is much harder architecturally.
07 Using the reparameterization formula, compute x_t for a pixel with x_0 = 0.5, ε = −0.8, and ᾱ_t = 0.64. Show the calculation. ▼
x_t = 0.4 − 0.48 = −0.08 (below 0 before clipping to display range).
Step 1: √ᾱ_t = √0.64 = 0.8.
Step 2: 1 − ᾱ_t = 0.36, so √(1−ᾱ_t) = √0.36 = 0.6.
Step 3: x_t = 0.8 × 0.5 + 0.6 × (−0.8) = 0.40 − 0.48 = −0.08.
The pixel was pushed below 0 by the noise (ε was negative and large enough to overcome the signal). In practice, values outside [0,1] occur during diffusion and are handled in the normalized latent space — only the final VAE decode clips to the valid pixel range.
08 DDIM (Song et al., 2020) reduces the 1000 reverse steps needed by DDPM to just 20–50. What algorithmic change enables this speedup? ▼
DDIM reformulates the reverse process as a non-Markovian ODE instead of a Markov chain, enabling larger, higher-order integration steps. DDPM's reverse process is stochastic (Markovian) — each step introduces new random noise, forcing small steps to stay accurate. DDIM's deterministic ODE-based formulation means consecutive denoising steps are consistent: you can skip ahead further in timestep without accumulating large integration error. Essentially, DDIM treats the reverse process like solving a differential equation and applies more efficient numerical solvers (similar to how ODE solvers use larger step sizes where the function is smooth). The same trained DDPM model can be used with DDIM sampling — no retraining required.
09 Why can't diffusion models be directly applied to text generation the way they are to image generation? ▼
Text is discrete — you cannot add Gaussian noise to a token ID and get a meaningful interpolation. In image diffusion, x_t = √ᾱ_t · x_0 + √(1−ᾱ_t) · ε works because pixel values are continuous real numbers — mixing a pixel value with Gaussian noise is well-defined. Token IDs (e.g., "cat" = 4231, "dog" = 5897) have no continuous geometry: the average of "cat" and "dog" is not "pet." Diffusion over discrete tokens would require defining a meaningful noise process on a discrete space, which is much harder. This is why LLMs use autoregressive generation instead — predicting the next token one at a time — which naturally handles the discrete token vocabulary.
