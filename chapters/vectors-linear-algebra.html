Vectors & Linear Algebra

The mathematical backbone of every neural network, embedding, and matrix operation in AI.

Every number a neural network ever processes is a vector. Every weight matrix is a linear transformation. The dot product between a query and a key is what decides what the transformer attends to. You cannot understand AI without this. Linear algebra is not background knowledge — it is the computation.

The Intuition

Two ways to see a vector — both are essential

A vector is simultaneously an arrow in space and a list of numbers. These are not two different objects — they are the same thing viewed from two complementary angles, and fluency in ML requires switching between them effortlessly.

As an arrow: a vector has magnitude (length) and direction. You can picture it. When you say "word2vec places 'king' near 'queen' in embedding space," you are using the arrow view — two points close together in a high-dimensional space, meaning their vectors point in similar directions.

As a list of numbers: a vector [3, -1, 2] is coordinates you can compute with. Add two lists elementwise, multiply by a scalar, feed them to a matrix — the arithmetic is what machines actually do.

The reason linearity is the default assumption in ML is not laziness — it is that linear operations are the only ones guaranteed to be:

  • Composable — two linear transformations composed are still linear. Layers stack.
  • Differentiable everywhere — critical for gradient descent, which requires gradients at every point.
  • Representable as matrix multiplication — which GPUs are designed to do at massive scale.
  • Well-understood — eigenvalues, rank, invertibility — we have complete theory for linear maps.

Non-linearities (ReLU, sigmoid) are added deliberately — in narrow doses, at specific points — to give the network expressive power beyond what any linear model can achieve. The non-linear part is the exception. The linear algebra is the rule.

Understanding It Deeply

Three questions to build genuine understanding

These three questions separate people who have memorized the formulas from people who understand what the formulas are doing. Work through each before reading the answer.

Think first The dot product formula is v · w = |v| |w| cos θ. If two vectors are perpendicular, their dot product is zero. If they point in the same direction, it equals the product of their magnitudes. What does this tell you geometrically about what a dot product actually measures?

The dot product measures how much one vector projects onto another. Specifically, v · w = |v| |w| cos θ is the length of v's shadow cast onto w, multiplied by |w|. When vectors align perfectly, the full length of v contributes — maximum dot product. When perpendicular, no shadow at all — zero. When pointing opposite directions, the projection is negative.

This is why dot products measure similarity in ML. Two embedding vectors with a large positive dot product represent semantically similar concepts — they "point in the same direction" in the meaning space. The attention mechanism in transformers is literally softmax(QKᵀ / √d) — a scaled dot product between queries and keys, measuring which keys are most aligned with each query.

Think first A matrix M multiplies a vector v to produce Mv. But why does this constitute a transformation of space rather than just "a formula that gives you a new vector"? What happens to the entire coordinate system, not just the single vector v?

Every matrix encodes a transformation of the entire space. When you compute Mv, you are applying that transformation to the point v lives in. The key insight: a matrix is defined by what it does to the basis vectors. The first column of M is where the unit vector [1,0] lands. The second column is where [0,1] lands. Every other vector's destination is determined by linearity — its transformed position is the linear combination of those column landings weighted by its components.

This is why weight matrices in neural networks are genuinely transformations: they rotate, scale, and shear the feature space. A well-trained layer finds a transformation that makes the classes linearly separable — it reorganizes points in the space until the next layer can draw a clean boundary.

Think first An eigenvector satisfies Av = λv — the matrix only scales it, never rotates it. Why is this special? What would it mean for a transformation to have no real eigenvectors? And what do eigenvalues tell you about a transformation that the matrix entries themselves do not make obvious?

Eigenvectors are the natural axes of a transformation — the directions the transformation "agrees to preserve." When you apply A repeatedly, everything eventually collapses toward the dominant eigenvector (the one with the largest |λ|). This is why power iteration works for finding dominant eigenvectors: just keep applying the matrix.

Eigenvalues reveal the transformation's character: if all |λ| < 1, the transformation contracts space to a point (stable). If any |λ| > 1, it expands in that direction (potentially unstable). The determinant is the product of all eigenvalues — it tells you how the transformation scales volume. A determinant of zero means at least one eigenvalue is zero, meaning the transformation collapses space to a lower dimension — the matrix is non-invertible, rank-deficient.

In PCA, eigenvectors of the covariance matrix are the principal components — the directions of maximum variance in the data. The corresponding eigenvalues are how much variance each direction captures. This is why PCA is simultaneously a linear algebra operation and a statistical technique.

Worked example: attention scores in a 2-token transformer

In a transformer, each token is represented as a vector. The attention mechanism asks: "how much should token 1 attend to token 2?" The answer comes from a scaled dot product between queries (Q) and keys (K). Here is the full computation for a 2-token sequence with 2D embeddings.

1 Input embeddings — two tokens, each a 2D vector:
token₁ = [1, 0], token₂ = [0, 1]
2 Project to Q and K with learned weight matrices Wq, Wk (using identity here for clarity):
Q = X·Wq = [[1,0],[0,1]], K = X·Wk = [[1,0],[0,1]]
3 Compute attention scores: QKᵀ — multiply Q by the transpose of K:
QKᵀ = [[1,0],[0,1]] × [[1,0],[0,1]]ᵀ = [[1,0],[0,1]]
Row i, column j = how much token i attends to token j. Diagonal = self-attention.
4 Scale and softmax: divide by √d = √2 ≈ 1.41, then softmax each row:
scaled = [[0.71, 0], [0, 0.71]]
softmax(row₁) = [0.675, 0.325] → token₁ attends 67.5% to itself
Scaling by √d prevents dot products from growing too large in high dimensions, keeping softmax in a useful gradient range.
Every attention score is a dot product — linear algebra, end to end. The entire attention mechanism is matrix multiplication: form Q, K, V projections, compute scaled dot products, apply softmax, aggregate with V. No loops, no explicit similarity function — just three matrix multiplies and a softmax. This is why transformers run efficiently on hardware built for matrix operations.

From Toy to Real

How linear algebra shows up in four real ML systems

The operations above are not abstract exercises. They are the literal computations happening inside every model you use.

Embeddings (word2vec, sentence transformers)

A word embedding maps a token to a vector in ℝ³⁰⁰ (or ℝ⁷⁶⁸). "King" becomes a point in 300-dimensional space. "Queen" is nearby. "Banana" is far. The famous king − man + woman ≈ queen relationship is vector arithmetic — addition and subtraction of 300-dimensional vectors. Cosine similarity (normalized dot product) is the standard measure of semantic proximity.

Weight matrices in neural networks

Every dense layer computes y = Wx + b — a matrix multiply followed by a vector addition. For a layer mapping 512 inputs to 256 outputs, W is a 256×512 matrix — 131,072 learned parameters. The entire forward pass of a network is a sequence of such matrix multiplications interleaved with non-linearities. Training adjusts W via gradient descent; backpropagation computes the gradient of the loss with respect to W using the chain rule applied to matrix calculus.

PCA and covariance matrices

PCA computes the eigendecomposition of the data covariance matrix C = (1/n)XᵀX. The eigenvectors of C are the principal components — directions of maximum variance. The eigenvalues are the variance along each direction. Projecting data onto the top k eigenvectors discards the k least-informative dimensions. This is exactly eigendecomposition applied to reduce the dimensionality of a feature space.

Attention matrices in transformers

As shown in the worked example, the attention mechanism is Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ/√d)V. The matrix QKᵀ is an n×n matrix of dot products between every pair of tokens — the full pairwise similarity structure of the sequence. This matrix is computed for every head, at every layer, for every batch — the dominant cost in transformer inference.

The common thread: In every case — embeddings, weight matrices, PCA, attention — the computation is a matrix operation on vectors. The semantic content lives in the numbers; the structure lives in linear algebra. When you optimize a model, you are searching through a space of matrices to find one whose transformations align with the patterns in your data.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs — and why they matter

These are not random mistakes. Each one follows from the previous, and each one leads to concrete wrong conclusions about how ML models work.

  • ① Matrices are just organized tables of numbers.
    A matrix is a linear transformation. Every matrix multiply is a rotation, scaling, shearing, or projection of space. Thinking of weight matrices as "tables of parameters" misses the geometric meaning entirely. When you multiply two matrices, you are composing two transformations — not multiplying two tables. This distinction matters when you reason about what a network layer is doing to the feature space.
  • ② Every matrix has an inverse you can use to "undo" it.
    A matrix is invertible if and only if its determinant is non-zero — equivalently, if it has full rank, meaning its rows (or columns) are linearly independent. A rank-deficient matrix maps multiple input points to the same output point: it destroys information. You cannot recover the input because the transformation was not one-to-one. Wide neural network layers with more inputs than outputs are always non-square and thus structurally non-invertible.
  • ③ High-dimensional intuition works like 2D or 3D intuition.
    Almost nothing in high-dimensional geometry matches low-dimensional intuition. In 100 dimensions, nearly all the volume of a unit ball is concentrated in a thin shell near the surface. Two random vectors are almost certainly nearly perpendicular (dot product ≈ 0). This is why cosine similarity works as an embedding measure — random high-dimensional vectors are already nearly orthogonal, so similarity scores near 1.0 are meaningful. The "curse of dimensionality" is real, and it comes directly from high-dimensional geometry.
  • ④ The dot product is just multiplication — it's like multiplying two numbers.
    The dot product produces a scalar that measures alignment of two vectors in space. Multiplying two numbers has no directional meaning. 3 × 5 = 15 tells you nothing about angles. [1,0] · [1,0] = 1 (perfectly aligned) vs [1,0] · [0,1] = 0 (perpendicular) tells you everything about their geometric relationship. In attention mechanisms, the dot product is a similarity measure, not a multiplication. The confusion leads to misunderstanding why scaling by √d is necessary (to prevent the dot products from growing too large in high dimensions).
  • ⑤ Eigenvectors are an exotic theoretical concept with no practical relevance to ML.
    PCA is eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix. The principal components are eigenvectors. Spectral methods in graph neural networks use eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian. Singular value decomposition (SVD) — used in recommendation systems, NLP, and model compression — is a generalization of eigendecomposition to non-square matrices. Understanding what eigenvectors are (directions a transformation leaves unchanged, scaled only) directly explains why PCA finds the "directions of maximum variance."

Summary

Six things to lock in

  • Vectors are both arrows (magnitude + direction) and lists of numbers — ML requires fluency in both views simultaneously.
  • The dot product measures alignment: v·w = |v||w|cos θ. It is the foundation of similarity search, attention, and projection.
  • A matrix is a linear transformation of space. Matrix multiply = compose two transformations. Column j of M shows where basis vector eⱼ lands.
  • Determinant = signed volume scaling factor. Zero determinant = rank-deficient = non-invertible = information destroyed.
  • Eigenvectors are the natural axes of a transformation — directions it scales but does not rotate. Eigenvalues are the scale factors. PCA finds them.
  • Every transformer forward pass is matrix multiplication: QKᵀ computes pairwise attention scores; the rest is scaling, softmax, and another matrix multiply with V.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: We know that training a neural network means adjusting millions of matrix entries to reduce loss. But how does the system know which direction to adjust each entry? The network must compute the rate of change of loss with respect to every single weight — a gradient in millions of dimensions. The machinery that makes this tractable is calculus applied to composed functions. That's what comes next: Calculus & Derivatives.

Test Your Understanding

10 Questions

0 / 10 revealed

01 Compute the dot product of v = [3, 4] and w = [1, −2]. What does the sign of the result tell you geometrically? ▼
v · w = (3)(1) + (4)(−2) = 3 − 8 = −5. The negative sign means the vectors form an obtuse angle — they point in "partially opposing" directions (cos θ < 0, so θ > 90°). The vectors have a negative projection onto each other. In a similarity context (embeddings), a negative dot product indicates dissimilarity — the representations are on "opposite sides" of the origin.
02 What is the L2 norm of the vector v = [3, 4]? Why is normalizing embeddings to unit vectors useful before computing dot products? ▼
‖v‖ = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5. When embeddings are normalized to unit length, the dot product equals the cosine: v̂ · ŵ = cos θ. This removes the effect of magnitude and measures only directional alignment — semantic similarity, not vector length. Without normalization, a long vector might have a large dot product with everything, inflating its apparent similarity. Cosine similarity is the standard for embedding retrieval precisely because it normalizes magnitude.
03 Multiply the matrices A = [[2, 1], [0, 3]] and B = [[1, 4], [2, 0]]. What does the result AB represent geometrically? ▼
AB = [[2·1+1·2, 2·4+1·0], [0·1+3·2, 0·4+3·0]] = [[4, 8], [6, 0]]. Geometrically, AB represents the composition of two transformations: first apply B, then apply A. If B shears space and A scales it, then AB does both in sequence. Matrix multiplication is not commutative (AB ≠ BA in general) because applying transformations in different orders produces different results — the order encodes which transformation acts first.
04 The determinant of a 2×2 matrix [[a, b], [c, d]] is ad − bc. What does a determinant of zero mean for the transformation the matrix represents? ▼
A zero determinant means the matrix collapses space to a lower dimension. In 2D, a det = 0 matrix squashes the entire plane onto a line (or a point). Multiple input points map to the same output point — information is destroyed and the transformation cannot be undone (the matrix is non-invertible). In the context of neural networks: a weight matrix with linearly dependent rows compresses the feature space, potentially losing the ability to represent certain patterns. Rank deficiency is the formal term; det = 0 is one diagnostic.
05 A vector v satisfies Av = 3v for matrix A. What is v called, what is 3 called, and what does this relationship mean for how A transforms the space near v? ▼
v is an eigenvector of A; 3 is the corresponding eigenvalue. The equation Av = 3v means that when you apply the transformation A to v, the result points in exactly the same direction as v — it is only scaled by a factor of 3. This means v identifies a "stable direction" of the transformation: points along v get stretched by 3×, but never rotated. In PCA, eigenvectors of the covariance matrix are the axes of maximum variance; the eigenvalue (3 here) tells you how much variance is captured in that direction.
06 In the transformer attention formula Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ/√d)V, why is the score matrix divided by √d? What happens if you skip this step? ▼
The scaling by √d prevents dot products from growing too large in high-dimensional spaces. With d-dimensional query and key vectors whose components are independent with mean 0 and variance 1, the dot product has expected value 0 but variance d. Without scaling, the dot products grow proportionally to d, pushing softmax inputs into regions with near-zero gradients (the softmax saturates — it returns a one-hot-like distribution that "pays attention" to one token with near-certainty, ignoring all others). Dividing by √d normalizes the variance back to 1, keeping softmax in a useful, gradient-preserving range. (Vaswani et al., §3.2.1)
07 You have a 512-dimensional word embedding for "cat" and another for "dog." You compute their cosine similarity. Is it likely to be closer to 0 or closer to 1, and why? ▼
Closer to 1 — "cat" and "dog" are semantically related animals. In a well-trained embedding space (word2vec, GloVe, sentence transformers), semantically similar words map to nearby vectors. "Cat" and "dog" share many linguistic contexts: they both appear near "pet," "fur," "veterinarian," etc. Words trained on similar contexts develop similar embeddings. The cosine similarity would be noticeably above zero — typically 0.6–0.8 for closely related concepts — whereas "cat" and "democracy" would be near zero (unrelated) and "cat" and its antonym (none exists literally) might be slightly negative.
08 What is the rank of a matrix, and why does a weight matrix with rank lower than its smaller dimension indicate a problem for a neural network layer? ▼
Rank is the number of linearly independent rows (or columns) — the true dimensionality of the transformation's output. A weight matrix W mapping ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ can have rank at most min(n, m). If rank(W) < min(n, m), the layer maps all inputs into a subspace smaller than its output dimension — it has a "bottleneck" tighter than its stated size. Information is compressed below what the architecture suggests. This matters during training: if a large layer is effectively low-rank, you're carrying many parameters that don't increase expressive power. This observation motivates LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), which explicitly parameterizes weight updates as low-rank matrices.
09 PCA "finds the directions of maximum variance in the data." Express this precisely: what is being computed, and what are the outputs? ▼
PCA computes the eigendecomposition of the data covariance matrix C = (1/n)XᵀX. The eigenvectors of C are the principal components — orthogonal directions in the original feature space that capture the most variance. The corresponding eigenvalues λ₁ ≥ λ₂ ≥ … ≥ λₙ measure how much variance each component captures. Projecting data onto the top k eigenvectors (the k largest eigenvalues) gives a k-dimensional representation that retains the most information in the least-squares sense. The fraction of variance explained by k components is (λ₁+…+λₖ)/(λ₁+…+λₙ).
10 Two random unit vectors are sampled uniformly from the surface of a 1000-dimensional sphere. Is their dot product likely to be close to 0, 1, or −1? Why does this matter for ML? ▼
Close to 0 — in high dimensions, random vectors are nearly orthogonal. With d-dimensional unit vectors, the expected dot product is 0 and the standard deviation is 1/√d ≈ 0.032 for d = 1000. Almost all pairs are nearly perpendicular. This has two major consequences for ML: (1) random initializations of weight matrices give near-zero dot products, which is good — all neurons start with near-zero mutual correlation. (2) A cosine similarity score of 0.7 in a 1000-dimensional embedding space is very significant — it means two vectors are far more aligned than random, genuinely capturing semantic similarity. This is the geometric foundation of why embedding-based similarity search works.
