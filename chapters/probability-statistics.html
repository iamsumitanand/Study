Probability & Statistics

The language every ML model speaks — whether it knows it or not.

← Calculus & Derivatives Information Theory →
Machine learning doesn't find the right answer — it finds the most probable answer. Every loss function is a likelihood. Every prediction is a distribution. Every weight update is a Bayesian update. Without probability, you can't understand what any model is actually doing.

The Intuition

A distribution is not a number — it's a story

There are two ways to think about probability. The frequentist view says probability is the long-run frequency of an event: flip a fair coin a million times, heads comes up ~50% of the time. The Bayesian view says probability is a degree of belief — a number that quantifies how certain you are about something, updated whenever new evidence arrives.

ML straddles both. Neural networks are often trained with maximum likelihood (frequentist framing), but interpreted with confidence scores and uncertainty estimates (Bayesian framing). The underlying math is the same either way — what changes is the story you tell about it.

  • A distribution assigns a probability to every possible outcome — it's the full picture, not a summary.
  • A statistic (mean, variance) collapses that picture into a single number — useful, but lossy.
  • When a model outputs 0.87, it's claiming: "I've placed probability 0.87 on this class." That is a distributional statement.
  • When you train with cross-entropy loss, you're maximizing the likelihood of the observed labels under your model's distribution.

The difference matters. Two distributions can have identical means and variances but completely different shapes. If you only look at summary statistics, you miss the full story the model is telling you.

Understanding It Deeply

Three ideas that underpin everything

The conceptual gaps below are where ML students stall. Work through each one before continuing. The animation section will make them visual.

Think first What is the difference between probability and likelihood — and why does it matter for MLE? Consider: given a Gaussian with fixed μ = 0, σ = 1, and a data point x = 2, is P(x = 2) the same as L(μ = 0 | x = 2)? What's being held fixed in each case?

They're the same formula evaluated from different directions. Probability is P(data | parameters) — the parameters are fixed, you ask how probable the data is. Likelihood is L(parameters | data) — the data is fixed, you ask how plausible the parameters are. This isn't just notation. In MLE, you fix the observed data and search over parameter space for the settings that make that data most probable. You're treating the data as given and parameters as the variable. In frequentist inference, you do the opposite. Same function, completely different inferential act.

Think first Why is the Gaussian distribution so ubiquitous in ML? Is it because real-world data is normally distributed? Or is there a deeper reason? Think about what happens when you average many independent random variables.

The Central Limit Theorem says: the mean of many independent, identically distributed random variables converges to a Gaussian, regardless of their underlying distribution — as long as the variance is finite. This makes the Gaussian a natural model for anything that arises as a sum of many small independent effects: measurement error, noise in sensor readings, residuals in regression. It also has a deep connection to maximum entropy — among all distributions with a fixed mean and variance, the Gaussian has the highest entropy. It's the least opinionated distribution consistent with those two constraints. Finally, Gaussians are computationally convenient: they're closed under addition, and their log-likelihood is a quadratic, which makes MLE equivalent to least squares.

Think first What does "conditional independence" mean, and why do naive Bayes models assume it? If two features are statistically dependent in reality — say, word counts for "free" and "money" in spam emails — does the naive Bayes assumption break the model completely?

Two random variables X and Y are conditionally independent given Z if knowing Z makes X and Y irrelevant to each other: P(X, Y | Z) = P(X | Z) · P(Y | Z). Naive Bayes assumes all features are conditionally independent given the class label. In text classification, this means word counts are independent given the class (spam vs. not spam). This is almost certainly wrong — but it rarely matters in practice. The assumption allows you to factor the joint likelihood into a product of per-feature likelihoods, making the model tractable with tiny datasets. The classifier often produces well-calibrated rankings even when the probability estimates themselves are badly off — because the relative ordering of class probabilities is often preserved even when the absolute values are wrong.

The Math

The core formulas — with meaning attached

Formulas without interpretation are just symbols. Each block below pairs the formula with a plain-English reading and a note on where it appears in ML.

Probability Axioms (Kolmogorov)
0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1  ·  P(Ω) = 1  ·  P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) if A ∩ B = ∅
P(A) probability of event A — a number in [0, 1] P(Ω) = 1all probabilities over the entire sample space sum to 1 — normalization A ∩ B = ∅A and B are mutually exclusive — cannot both occur
Conditional Probability
P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)
P(A | B) probability of A given that B has occurred P(A ∩ B) joint probability — both A and B occur P(B) marginal probability of B — the normalizer that ensures P(·|B) sums to 1
Bayes' Theorem
P(θ | data) = P(data | θ) · P(θ) / P(data)
P(θ | data) posterior — belief about parameters after seeing data P(data | θ) likelihood — how probable the data is under parameter θ P(θ) prior — belief about θ before seeing any data P(data) evidence — normalizing constant (often intractable; ignored in MAP)
Expectation and Variance
E[X] = Σ x · P(X=x)  ·  Var(X) = E[(X − E[X])²] = E[X²] − (E[X])²
E[X] expected value — weighted average of outcomes, weighted by probability Var(X) variance — expected squared deviation from the mean; measures spread σ = √Varstandard deviation — same units as X, easier to interpret than variance
Gaussian (Normal) PDF
f(x; μ, σ) = (1 / σ√2π) · exp(−(x−μ)² / 2σ²)
μ mean — center of the distribution; shifts the bell curve left/right σ standard deviation — controls width; 68% of mass lies in [μ−σ, μ+σ] exp the exponential ensures the function is always positive and integrates to 1
MLE Objective
θ̂ = argmax_θ Σᵢ log P(xᵢ | θ)
θ̂ MLE estimate — the parameter value that makes the observed data most likely log P log-likelihood — log turns products into sums; easier to differentiate Σᵢ log P sum over all data points — assumes i.i.d. samples so joint = product of marginals

Worked example — binary classification with Bayes' theorem.

A spam filter sees the email "get free money now". Compute P(spam | "free money") from known priors and likelihoods.

1 Establish the prior. From historical data, 30% of emails are spam: P(spam) = 0.30, P(ham) = 0.70. This is our belief before reading the words.
2 Estimate the likelihoods. In the spam training corpus, the phrase "free money" appears in 40% of spam emails: P("free money" | spam) = 0.40. In ham, it appears in 2%: P("free money" | ham) = 0.02.
3 Compute the evidence. P("free money") = P("free money" | spam) · P(spam) + P("free money" | ham) · P(ham) = 0.40 × 0.30 + 0.02 × 0.70 = 0.12 + 0.014 = 0.134
4 Apply Bayes' theorem. P(spam | "free money") = (0.40 × 0.30) / 0.134 = 0.12 / 0.134 ≈ 0.896. The email has an 89.6% posterior probability of being spam. The prior was 30% — the evidence moved it dramatically.

From Theory to Real Models

Probability is the hidden architecture of every ML model

Most ML models don't look like probability models. But look at what they're actually optimizing and you'll find probability everywhere.

Logistic regression

Despite the name, logistic regression is a probabilistic classifier. It models P(y = 1 | x) = σ(wᵀx + b), where σ is the sigmoid function that squashes any real number into (0, 1). Training with binary cross-entropy loss is exactly maximum likelihood estimation under a Bernoulli distribution. The model is explicitly outputting a probability — not a score, not a distance, a calibrated probability.

Gaussian assumptions in linear regression

Ordinary least squares (OLS) minimizes the sum of squared residuals. Where does that come from? Assume residuals are i.i.d. Gaussian noise: εᵢ ~ N(0, σ²). Then the log-likelihood of the data is the negative of the sum of squared residuals — so MLE under Gaussian noise is exactly OLS. The squared loss isn't arbitrary. It's the signature of a Gaussian noise assumption.

Bayesian neural networks

Standard neural networks give point estimates of weights. Bayesian NNs maintain a distribution over weights: P(w | data) ∝ P(data | w) · P(w). This is expensive to compute exactly, so variational inference approximates it. The payoff: the model can express uncertainty — instead of outputting a single prediction, it outputs a distribution over predictions. This is critical for safety-critical applications (medical diagnosis, autonomous vehicles) where knowing what the model doesn't know matters.

Uncertainty quantification

Modern ML distinguishes two types of uncertainty. Aleatoric uncertainty is irreducible noise in the data itself — more data won't help. Epistemic uncertainty is due to limited training data — adding more data reduces it. Probability theory is the language for distinguishing and quantifying both.

When a model outputs a single number — a price, a score, a label — it has collapsed a probability distribution into a point estimate. Understanding that collapse, and what was lost in it, is the difference between using a model and understanding one.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs about probability in ML

These aren't disconnected mistakes. They form a chain — each one leads naturally to the next. Trace the chain and they all become impossible to hold.

  • ① Probability just means frequency.
    Frequentist probability (long-run frequency) is one interpretation — not the only one. Bayesian probability is a degree of belief, updated by evidence. When a model outputs P(rain tomorrow) = 0.73, there is no "long run" of identical tomorrows. The number is a calibrated belief, not a frequency. Most ML inference is Bayesian in spirit, even when trained with frequentist methods.
  • ② The Gaussian is always the right distribution to assume.
    The Gaussian is mathematically convenient and justified by CLT in many settings — but real data can have heavy tails (financial returns), be bounded (probabilities, counts), or be multimodal (mixture distributions). Assuming Gaussian when data is heavy-tailed systematically underestimates the probability of extreme events. The squared loss in regression is Gaussian noise; if your residuals look heavy-tailed, you should use a different loss (Huber, Laplace).
  • ③ More data means our distribution estimate is correct.
    More data reduces estimation error for the parameters of your assumed model — but if the model family is wrong (you're fitting a Gaussian to multimodal data), infinite data just makes you more precisely wrong. Model mis-specification is a separate problem from statistical efficiency. "We have a lot of data" does not mean "our probabilistic model is correct."
  • ④ Correlation means dependence.
    Zero correlation does not mean independence. Correlation measures only linear relationships. X and X² have zero Pearson correlation when X ~ N(0, 1) — but X² is completely determined by X. They are perfectly dependent, not independent. In contrast, joint normality plus zero correlation does imply independence — but only for Gaussians. Naive Bayes's conditional independence assumption is about statistical independence, which is much stronger than zero correlation.
  • ⑤ The p-value tells you the probability that your hypothesis is wrong.
    P(data | H₀) is not P(H₀ | data). A p-value is the probability of seeing data this extreme if the null hypothesis were true. It says nothing about the probability that the null is true or false. Inverting this — "p = 0.03, so there's only a 3% chance I'm wrong" — is a textbook fallacy called the prosecutor's fallacy. This matters in ML when evaluating model significance: a low p-value means the result is unlikely under the null, not that the model is definitely good.

Summary

Six things to lock in

  • Probability and likelihood use the same formula in opposite directions: P(data | θ) fixes θ; L(θ | data) fixes data. MLE searches over θ.
  • Bayes' theorem: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior. Every update moves your belief in proportion to the evidence.
  • The Gaussian is ubiquitous because of CLT (sums of small effects), maximum entropy (least opinionated at fixed mean/variance), and computational convenience (MLE = OLS).
  • Conditional independence P(X,Y|Z) = P(X|Z)P(Y|Z) is what makes naive Bayes tractable. It's usually false but often harmless in practice.
  • Cross-entropy loss is MLE under a categorical distribution. Squared loss is MLE under a Gaussian noise model. The loss function encodes a distributional assumption.
  • Correlation ≠ independence. Zero correlation means no linear dependence. True statistical independence is strictly stronger.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: You now know how to measure uncertainty with probability. But how do you measure the amount of information in a probability distribution? How can two distributions with the same variance contain different amounts of information? And why does the optimal code length for a message equal its log-probability?

That's information theory — and it's where entropy, KL divergence, and cross-entropy loss all come from. It's the next chapter.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 A Gaussian has μ = 0, σ = 1. You observe x = 2. Is L(μ=0 | x=2) the same number as P(x=2 | μ=0, σ=1)? What's the conceptual difference? ▼
They're numerically identical — but conceptually opposite. Both equal the Gaussian PDF evaluated at x=2: (1/√2π) exp(−2). The difference is what's variable: in P(x=2 | μ=0, σ=1), the parameters are fixed and x is the outcome; in L(μ=0 | x=2), x is fixed (it's your data) and μ is the hypothesis you're evaluating. MLE finds the μ that maximizes L(μ | x=2) — it treats the observation as given and searches over parameter space.
02 P(spam) = 0.25, P("free" | spam) = 0.6, P("free" | ham) = 0.05. A new email contains "free". What is P(spam | "free")? ▼
P(spam | "free") ≈ 0.80. Evidence: P("free") = 0.6 × 0.25 + 0.05 × 0.75 = 0.15 + 0.0375 = 0.1875. Posterior: (0.6 × 0.25) / 0.1875 = 0.15 / 0.1875 = 0.80. The prior was 25%; one word moved it to 80%. This demonstrates how strongly even a single informative feature can shift a Bayesian belief.
03 You double σ in a Gaussian. What happens to the peak height of the PDF, and why? Does the area under the curve change? ▼
The peak height halves; the area stays exactly 1. The peak of a Gaussian is 1/(σ√2π) — so doubling σ halves the peak. But the PDF must integrate to 1 (it's a valid probability distribution), so the curve flattens and widens to compensate. The distribution spreads out, placing more probability mass in the tails. This is why σ controls uncertainty: larger σ means the distribution is more diffuse, more probability assigned to values far from the mean.
04 Why does training with cross-entropy loss correspond to MLE? What distribution does it assume? ▼
Cross-entropy loss is negative log-likelihood under a categorical distribution. For a K-class problem with true label y and predicted probabilities p: cross-entropy = −log p(y). Summing over n examples: −Σᵢ log p(yᵢ). Maximizing the log-likelihood is identical to minimizing the cross-entropy. The distribution assumed is categorical (for classification) or Bernoulli (binary). This connection is why neural network classifiers trained with cross-entropy produce calibrated probabilities — in principle.
05 Two features X and Y have Pearson correlation = 0. Does this mean they are independent? Give a concrete counterexample. ▼
No. Zero correlation implies independence only for jointly Gaussian variables. Counterexample: let X ~ N(0,1) and Y = X². Cov(X, Y) = E[XY] − E[X]E[Y] = E[X³] − 0 = 0 (odd moments of symmetric distributions vanish). So Pearson correlation is exactly 0. But Y is completely determined by X — they are maximally dependent. Correlation measures only the linear component of dependence. Naive Bayes's conditional independence assumption is strictly stronger than assuming zero correlation.
06 Why does OLS (ordinary least squares) minimize the sum of squared residuals? What distributional assumption makes this the optimal thing to do? ▼
OLS is MLE under Gaussian noise. Assume yᵢ = wᵀxᵢ + εᵢ where εᵢ ~ N(0, σ²) i.i.d. The log-likelihood is: log L = −n log(σ√2π) − (1/2σ²) Σᵢ (yᵢ − wᵀxᵢ)². Maximizing over w is equivalent to minimizing Σᵢ (yᵢ − wᵀxᵢ)² — the sum of squared residuals. The squared loss isn't arbitrary; it's the direct consequence of assuming Gaussian residuals. If residuals are heavy-tailed (Laplace), the optimal loss is the absolute value (L1), not squared.
07 A naive Bayes classifier is told to classify a new document. Its predicted P(spam) = 1.000 with machine precision. Why is this a problem, and how do you fix it? ▼
Probability zero / one is catastrophic — and is caused by zero counts in training. If any word in the test document never appeared in spam training data, its likelihood P(word | spam) = 0, making the entire product zero regardless of other words. This is called the "zero-frequency problem." The fix is Laplace smoothing (add-1 smoothing): add a pseudocount of 1 to every word count, so no probability is ever exactly 0. This is a form of regularization with a uniform Dirichlet prior — a Bayesian interpretation.
08 What is the difference between aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty? Which one can you reduce by collecting more data? ▼
Only epistemic uncertainty decreases with more data. Aleatoric uncertainty is irreducible noise in the data itself — inherent randomness in the world. If a fair die always produces random outcomes, more rolls don't reduce your uncertainty about the next roll. Epistemic uncertainty stems from limited knowledge — you don't have enough data to pin down the model parameters. More data shrinks the posterior distribution over parameters, reducing this form of uncertainty. Distinguishing the two matters for model deployment: if high uncertainty is aleatoric, getting more data won't help; if it's epistemic, it will.
09 A model reports p-value = 0.02 for a new feature. A colleague says "there's only a 2% chance this feature is useless." Is that correct? ▼
No. That's the prosecutor's fallacy. A p-value is P(data this extreme | H₀ is true), not P(H₀ | data). p = 0.02 means: if the feature truly had no effect, there's a 2% chance of observing data this extreme by chance. It says nothing about the probability that the null hypothesis is true. Correctly: "if the null were true, results this strong would occur only 2% of the time by chance." To make probability statements about hypotheses, you need a Bayesian framework with an explicit prior over hypotheses.
