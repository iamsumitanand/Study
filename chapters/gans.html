GANs

Two neural networks locked in an adversarial game — each sharpening the other.

← Autoencoders Diffusion Models →
A counterfeiter and a detective locked in an arms race — each getting better only because the other exists. The counterfeiter studies every rejection to forge more convincing fakes. The detective studies every successful forgery to sharpen their eye. Neither improves in isolation. That's a GAN.

The Intuition

A two-player game where both players are neural networks

A Generative Adversarial Network is, at its core, a minimax game between two networks — the Generator and the Discriminator — trained simultaneously against each other.

The Generator takes random noise as input and produces synthetic data — images, audio, text. It has never seen the real data directly. Its only signal is the Discriminator's verdict: real or fake.

The Discriminator sees a mix of real samples and Generator-produced fakes. Its job is binary classification: assign high probability to real data, low probability to fakes.

  • The Generator wants to fool the Discriminator — push D(G(z)) toward 1 (looks real).
  • The Discriminator wants to resist — keep D(x) near 1 for real, D(G(z)) near 0 for fakes.
  • The Nash equilibrium is the theoretical end state: G produces samples indistinguishable from real data, and D is forced to output 0.5 everywhere — pure uncertainty.
  • In practice the game never perfectly converges, but the pressure from both sides is what drives G toward realism.

The key insight from the original Goodfellow et al. (2014) paper: you don't need to explicitly define what a "good" generated image looks like. You just need a Discriminator willing to enforce the standard — and let the Generator's adversarial pressure do the rest.

Understanding It Deeply

Three hard problems that took the field years to solve

GANs are deceptively simple to describe and notoriously difficult to train. Three failure modes dominate the literature — each with a distinct cause and a different fix.

Think first The original GAN loss for the Generator is: minimize E[log(1 − D(G(z)))]. Early in training, D easily rejects G's crude fakes — D(G(z)) ≈ 0, so log(1 − D(G(z))) ≈ log(1) ≈ 0. What happens to the gradient signal for G in this situation?

The gradient vanishes. When D(G(z)) is close to 0, the function log(1 − D(G(z))) is nearly flat — its gradient with respect to G's parameters is essentially zero. G receives no useful training signal precisely when it needs it most (at the start, when its outputs are worst). The standard fix — proposed in the original paper — is to instead maximize E[log D(G(z))], which has strong gradients when D(G(z)) is near 0. This "non-saturating" loss is what most implementations actually use, though the theoretical minimax formulation is still written as the min-max objective.

Think first Suppose the Generator discovers that one particular output — say, a blurry image of a single face — consistently fools the Discriminator with probability 0.8. What does gradient descent encourage the Generator to do next?

It keeps producing that same face. And more of it. And only it. This is mode collapse — the Generator converges to a single mode (or small set of modes) of the data distribution, ignoring the rest. The Generator is optimizing its objective perfectly; the problem is the objective doesn't penalize lack of diversity. The Discriminator eventually catches on and rejects that face, but by then G has already overfit to a different small cluster. The two networks chase each other in circles. Fixes include minibatch discrimination (letting D see batches rather than individual samples), Wasserstein loss, and progressive growing.

Think first Diffusion models now generate more photorealistic and diverse images than most GANs. GANs had a 7-year head start (2014–2021). Why did GANs dominate image generation for so long before being displaced?

Two reasons. First, inference speed: a trained GAN generates an image in a single forward pass through G — microseconds. Diffusion models require hundreds of denoising steps at inference, making them far slower to sample from. Second, training data efficiency: GANs can produce sharp, high-resolution images with relatively small datasets. StyleGAN2 was trained on ~70k images for faces; equivalent diffusion models need far larger corpora. GANs also had a compounding advantage — each new architecture (DCGAN → WGAN → ProGAN → StyleGAN) built on working prior art. Diffusion models needed theoretical breakthroughs (score matching, DDPM, DDIM) before becoming competitive. The inflection point was 2021–2022, when DALL-E 2 and Stable Diffusion demonstrated that diffusion's quality and diversity advantages outweighed its speed cost, especially for text-conditional generation.

The Math

The minimax objective

The GAN objective is a two-player minimax game. D wants to maximize it; G wants to minimize it. Both gradients exist — they're just used in opposite directions.

GAN Minimax Objective
minG maxD 𝔼[log D(x)] + 𝔼[log(1 − D(G(z)))]
x a real sample drawn from the true data distribution pdata z random noise drawn from a simple prior (e.g., Gaussian) — G's input G(z) a fake sample produced by the Generator from noise z D(x) Discriminator's probability estimate that x is real (wants this near 1 for real data) D(G(z)) Discriminator's estimate that G's fake is real (D wants near 0; G wants near 1)

Reading the objective. The first term, E[log D(x)], is maximized when D correctly assigns high probability to real samples — this only involves D. The second term, E[log(1 − D(G(z)))], is where the competition lives: D maximizes it by assigning low D(G(z)) to fakes; G minimizes it by producing fakes that push D(G(z)) toward 1. Both networks differentiate through this shared term but pull in opposite directions.

Four-step worked example — two real, two fake samples:

Setup: 2 real samples, 2 fakes. Current D outputs: D(x₁)=0.9, D(x₂)=0.85, D(G(z₁))=0.3, D(G(z₂))=0.4
1 D loss (real term):  −(log 0.9 + log 0.85) / 2 = −(−0.105 − 0.163) / 2 = 0.134
2 D loss (fake term):  −(log(1−0.3) + log(1−0.4)) / 2 = −(log 0.7 + log 0.6) / 2 = 0.267
3 G loss (non-saturating):  −(log D(G(z₁)) + log D(G(z₂))) / 2 = −(log 0.3 + log 0.4) / 2 = 0.977
4 Equilibrium signal:  At Nash eq., D(G(z)) → 0.5, so G loss → −log(0.5) = log(2) ≈ 0.693. Both losses converge here.
Training loop: D steps first (1–2 gradient updates), then G steps once. Repeat. Updating D more frequently than G prevents G from improving faster than D can detect — a key stability trick from the original paper.

From Theory to Practice

What GANs actually built

The minimax objective above is from 2014. By 2021, the same idea had produced some of the most remarkable generative systems ever built.

StyleGAN (NVIDIA, 2019–2021). The architecture that made photorealistic synthetic faces mainstream. StyleGAN introduced a style-based generator where noise is mapped to an intermediate latent space W, and styles are injected at each resolution level via adaptive instance normalization. The result: controllable disentanglement of high-level attributes (pose, identity) from fine details (freckles, hair texture). The site thispersondoesnotexist.com runs StyleGAN2 — every face is fully synthetic, none is a real person.

CycleGAN (Berkeley, 2017). Unpaired image-to-image translation — translating between domains (horses ↔ zebras, photos ↔ paintings) without any paired training data. Uses two generators and two discriminators with a cycle-consistency loss: if you translate a horse to a zebra and back, you should recover the original horse. Enabled use cases where paired datasets don't exist (medical imaging domains, historical photo colorization).

Data augmentation for rare events. In domains where real data is scarce — rare disease pathology, satellite imagery of unusual weather, industrial defect detection — GANs generate synthetic training data to augment real datasets. This is now standard practice in medical imaging research, where a GAN trained on a few hundred MRI scans can synthesize thousands of plausible variants for downstream classifiers.

The core transferable pattern: anywhere you have a quality signal but lack the ability to define it analytically, an adversarial discriminator can learn that signal empirically. GANs made "I know it when I see it" into a differentiable training objective.

Common Misconceptions

Five causal misconceptions about GANs

These aren't random mistakes — they form a causal chain. Each one follows plausibly from the last.

  • ① The Generator learns by seeing real data and copying it.
    The Generator never sees real data directly. Its only training signal is the Discriminator's verdict on its outputs. G learns entirely through adversarial feedback — gradient from D's classification loss, backpropagated through D and into G. This is why GANs can generalize beyond mere copying: G must internalize the distribution, not memorize specific samples.
  • ② So the Discriminator is just a loss function for the Generator.
    The Discriminator is itself a neural network being trained simultaneously. It's not a fixed oracle — it evolves with G. A better G forces D to become more discriminating; a better D forces G to become more convincing. The adversarial dynamic requires both networks to be trained, not just G. Freezing D turns the objective into an ordinary supervised loss, losing the game-theoretic pressure entirely.
  • ③ Then GANs always converge to the Nash equilibrium where D outputs 0.5.
    The Nash equilibrium is a theoretical guarantee under the non-parametric assumption of infinite-capacity networks. Real GANs use finite networks trained with gradient descent, which has no convergence guarantee in non-convex, non-concave games. In practice, training frequently oscillates, mode-collapses, or diverges. The equilibrium is a useful north star, not a practical promise. Most research in GAN training (WGAN, spectral normalization, gradient penalty) is specifically about coaxing real training closer to equilibrium.
  • ④ Mode collapse happens because the Generator is undertrained relative to the Discriminator.
    Mode collapse is more subtle than a capacity or training ratio imbalance. It can occur even with perfectly balanced training because the Generator's optimization landscape has local minima where a small set of outputs reliably fools D. G exploits these optima greedily. The root cause is that the JS divergence (implicit in the original GAN loss) provides no gradient signal for missing modes — it's maximally penalized for fakes D rejects, but not for real modes G ignores. Wasserstein GAN fixes this by using a metric (Earth Mover's Distance) that rewards covering the full support of the real distribution.
  • ⑤ Diffusion models replaced GANs because they're fundamentally a better framework for generation.
    GANs still outperform diffusion models in several important dimensions: single-pass inference speed (no iterative denoising), training data efficiency, and fine-grained latent space control (StyleGAN's W space is remarkably well-structured). Diffusion models won on diversity, text-conditional fidelity, and training stability. The displacement was task-specific: for open-vocabulary text-to-image generation, diffusion models are clearly superior. For video generation, low-latency synthesis, and domain-specific applications with limited data, GANs remain highly competitive.

Summary

Six things to lock in

  • GAN = two networks (Generator + Discriminator) trained simultaneously via a minimax objective. G never sees real data; D never sees G's internals.
  • The Generator maps noise z → fake sample G(z). The Discriminator maps any sample → probability of being real. The adversarial signal flows from D back into G.
  • Nash equilibrium (theoretical): G perfectly models pdata, D outputs 0.5 everywhere. In practice, gradient descent in this non-convex game doesn't guarantee convergence.
  • Vanishing gradients early in training: original log(1 − D(G(z))) loss saturates. Fix: train G to maximize log D(G(z)) instead (non-saturating loss).
  • Mode collapse: G fixates on a subset of modes that reliably fool D. Root cause is JS divergence's blindness to missing modes. Fix: Wasserstein loss, minibatch discrimination.
  • GANs dominated image generation 2014–2021. Diffusion models surpassed them in diversity and text-conditioning, but GANs retain speed, data-efficiency, and structured latent-space advantages.
One question this chapter leaves open: GANs generate by learning to fool a discriminator. Diffusion models generate by learning to reverse a noise process — no adversary, no fragile game dynamics. How does adding noise progressively and then learning to remove it produce images that are more diverse and stable than adversarial training?

That's the next chapter: Diffusion Models. Same goal — sampling from pdata — completely different machinery.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 The Generator's loss is increasing but the Discriminator's accuracy is also increasing. What is happening, and is it a problem? ▼
This is normal early training behavior — and it's not immediately a problem. As D improves at distinguishing real from fake, G's loss rises because D rejects G's outputs more confidently. This is the adversarial signal working as intended: D's improvement creates harder targets for G, forcing G to produce better fakes. It becomes a problem only if D improves so rapidly that G's gradient vanishes (D(G(z)) → 0, causing log saturation). The fix in that case is the non-saturating loss — maximize log D(G(z)) rather than minimize log(1 − D(G(z))).
02 At Nash equilibrium, what value does D(x) output for every input x — both real and fake? Why? ▼
D(x) = 0.5 everywhere. At equilibrium, G perfectly models pdata, so fake and real samples are drawn from identical distributions. No classifier can do better than random guessing on identically distributed data — any output other than 0.5 would be exploitable by one player. The Goodfellow 2014 paper proves this formally: the optimal discriminator given a fixed G is D*(x) = pdata(x) / (pdata(x) + pG(x)), which equals 0.5 when pG = pdata.
03 You train a GAN on handwritten digits (0–9). After training, the Generator only ever produces 3s and 7s — very convincingly. Name the failure mode and its root cause. ▼
Mode collapse. The Generator has converged to two modes (3 and 7) of the true data distribution while ignoring the other eight. Root cause: the JS divergence implicit in the original GAN loss gives zero gradient signal for modes the Generator never attempts to produce. Once G discovers that 3s and 7s reliably fool D, gradient descent has no incentive to also produce 1s and 9s — they're simply absent from G's loss landscape. Fix: Wasserstein GAN measures Earth Mover's Distance, which penalizes missing modes, or minibatch discrimination which lets D detect statistical absence of diversity.
04 CycleGAN translates images between two domains (e.g., horses → zebras) without paired training data. What prevents it from simply mapping every horse image to the same zebra? ▼
The cycle-consistency loss. CycleGAN trains two generators: G (horse → zebra) and F (zebra → horse). The cycle loss requires F(G(x)) ≈ x — translating a horse to a zebra and back should recover the original horse. This forces G to preserve the horse's structure (pose, background, shape) rather than collapse all inputs to a single convincing zebra. Without cycle-consistency, G could ignore the input entirely and produce any plausible zebra, since there's no paired ground truth to compare against. The cycle acts as a structural regularizer.
05 Why is it typically recommended to update D multiple times (k steps) for every single G update? ▼
To maintain a strong, informative gradient signal for G. If G updates as fast as D, G can outpace D's ability to detect fakes — D provides an unreliable signal, and G receives misleading gradients. Keeping D slightly ahead ensures it remains a reliable quality critic. The original Goodfellow paper recommends k = 1 as a practical compromise (more D updates per G update is theoretically better, but expensive). WGAN-GP typically uses k = 5 (5 D steps per G step). The key insight: D's job is to provide a meaningful training signal; if D is worse than G, that signal degenerates.
06 StyleGAN can generate photorealistic faces with precise attribute control (age, gender, expression). What architectural choice enables this fine-grained control? ▼
The intermediate latent space W and style injection via AdaIN. Instead of feeding noise z directly to the generator, StyleGAN maps z to an intermediate W space via a mapping network (8 fully-connected layers). Vectors in W control "styles" — they're injected at each resolution layer via Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN), which scales and shifts feature maps. Because W is learned rather than fixed (like z's Gaussian prior), it naturally disentangles: moving in one direction of W changes pose, another changes age, another changes hair — without entanglement. This is why W is used for most GAN inversion and editing tasks.
07 A researcher argues that a GAN with a perfect Discriminator would be the ideal setup for training G. Is this correct? ▼
No — a perfect D destroys G's training signal. If D perfectly classifies every real and fake sample from the start, D(G(z)) ≈ 0 for all G outputs. The original GAN loss log(1 − D(G(z))) ≈ log(1) ≈ 0, and its gradient with respect to G's parameters ≈ 0. G learns nothing. A good D should be slightly better than G — discriminating enough to signal G's failures, but not so perfectly accurate that the gradient vanishes. This is precisely why alternating updates with balanced learning rates matter: the adversary needs to be a credible but not unbeatable opponent.
08 You use a GAN to generate synthetic X-ray images for a rare disease classifier because you only have 200 real examples. What key risk should you evaluate before using synthetic data for training? ▼
Training bias from mode collapse and distribution shift. If the GAN has mode-collapsed on the 200 real examples — producing only the most common presentation of the disease — synthetic samples will undersample rare variants. A classifier trained on this data will be biased toward the frequent presentation and miss atypical cases, potentially more dangerous than having less data. You should: (1) visually inspect generated samples for diversity, (2) compute FID/IS scores, (3) evaluate the downstream classifier on a held-out real test set — not synthetic — as the ground truth. Synthetic augmentation should supplement, not replace, evaluation on real data.
09 Diffusion models now dominate text-to-image generation. Name two specific tasks where GANs are still the better engineering choice, and explain why for each. ▼
1. Real-time / low-latency generation. GANs generate in a single forward pass — nanoseconds to milliseconds. Diffusion models require 20–1000 denoising steps at inference, making interactive applications (game asset generation, real-time video effects, streaming synthesis) impractical with diffusion. Distilled diffusion (consistency models, LCM) partially closes this gap, but GANs remain faster.

2. Limited data domains. StyleGAN2 produces high-quality faces from ~70k images. Diffusion models for comparable quality typically require millions of training samples. For domain-specific generation with small datasets (medical imaging, industrial defects, specialized art styles), GANs' sample efficiency makes them the practical choice.
