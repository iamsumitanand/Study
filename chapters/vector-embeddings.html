Vector Embeddings

How neural networks encode meaning as geometry — the concept underlying every modern AI system.

The word "king" minus the word "man" plus the word "woman" equals the word "queen". Literally, as vectors. Not metaphorically — you can compute it with arithmetic. This is what makes modern AI work: meaning encoded as geometry, relationships encoded as direction and distance.

The Intuition

Numbers as meaning: a geometry of concepts

Before embeddings, the standard way to represent words was a one-hot vector: a vector with a 1 at the word's index and 0 everywhere else. A vocabulary of 50,000 words meant 50,000-dimensional vectors, and "cat" and "dog" were exactly as far apart as "cat" and "democracy" — the encoding knew nothing about meaning.

Embeddings solve this by replacing that brittle, high-dimensional lookup with a dense, low-dimensional vector — typically 100 to 1536 numbers — where position carries semantic information. Words used in similar contexts end up at similar positions in this space. The geometry of the space reflects the geometry of meaning.

  • The position of a word in embedding space encodes its meaning and usage patterns.
  • The distance between two embeddings reflects semantic similarity — near = similar.
  • The direction of a vector offset captures a relationship — "is-male-of", "is-capital-of", "is-plural-of".
  • The same concept works for images, audio, code, molecules — anything a neural network can process.

This is fundamentally different from one-hot encoding in the same way a map is different from a phone book. A phone book lists things. A map encodes relationships between them.

Understanding It Deeply

Three questions worth stopping to think about

Think first Why does neural network training naturally produce meaningful geometry in embedding space? Nothing in the training objective says "make 'cat' and 'dog' nearby." So why does it happen?

Because meaning is encoded in context. Words used in similar sentences — "the cat sat," "the dog sat," "the cat ran," "the dog ran" — will develop similar embeddings because the network learns to predict the same kinds of neighbors. The training signal is prediction error, not a semantic label. Semantic geometry emerges as a side effect of learning to predict well. The network discovers that grouping similar things together is useful for making accurate predictions, so it does.

Think first What does cosine similarity measure that Euclidean distance misses? When would they give opposite answers?

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors — the direction they point — ignoring their magnitudes. Euclidean distance measures how far apart the vector tips are. Imagine two documents about cats: one short, one long. The long one has much larger values (more word occurrences), so its embedding vector is much longer. Euclidean distance says they're far apart. Cosine similarity says they're nearly identical, because they point in the same direction. In semantic search, you care about direction — what is this about? — not magnitude — how much text is there? This is why cosine similarity is the default in embedding systems.

Think first Why do embeddings from different models — even models trained on the same data — live in incompatible spaces? And what problem does alignment solve?

Each model finds its own embedding space through gradient descent, and there are infinitely many spaces that work equally well. Two models might learn that "cat" and "dog" are close, but one puts them in the upper-right region and the other puts them near the origin pointing left. The internal coordinates are arbitrary — only the relative geometry matters. This is why you can't mix embeddings across models: OpenAI's text embeddings and Cohere's embeddings are geometrically incompatible.

Alignment solves this by learning a mapping from one embedding space to another. If you have paired examples ("this French sentence means the same as this English sentence"), you can train a transformation that rotates and scales one space to match the other. This is how multilingual models and cross-modal systems (text + images) are built — not by creating a single universal space, but by learning to translate between spaces.

The Math

From one-hot to dense — and back to meaning

Plain English first: an embedding is a lookup table. You pass in a word index; you get out a vector of floats. During training, those floats are learned by gradient descent — the same update rule you saw in Part 2. The magic isn't the lookup. It's what the gradients push the numbers toward.

One-Hot vs Dense Embedding
one-hot("cat") = [0, 0, 1, 0, …, 0] ← 50,000-dimensional, all zeros except one
embed("cat") = E[i_cat] ← row i of embedding matrix E ∈ ℝ^(V×d)
V vocabulary size (e.g. 50,000) d embedding dimension (e.g. 300) — a hyperparameter, much smaller than V E[i] the i-th row of the embedding matrix — that word's vector i_catinteger index of "cat" in the vocabulary
Embedding Lookup as Matrix Multiply
embed(w) = one-hot(w) · E
one-hot(w)a row vector of length V with a single 1 at position w E embedding matrix of shape (V × d) result a row vector of length d — the word's dense embedding
Cosine Similarity
cos(a, b) = (a · b) / (‖a‖ · ‖b‖)
a · b dot product — sum of element-wise products ‖a‖ L2 norm of a — sqrt(Σ aᵢ²) result −1 (opposite directions) to +1 (same direction), 0 = orthogonal
Word2Vec Skip-Gram Objective
maximize Σ Σ log P(context_word | target_word)
target_word the word we're predicting from context_word words appearing within a window around the target P(c|t) softmax(embed(t) · embed(c)) — high when the two embeddings align

Worked example: the king − man + woman analogy in 3D

Assume a simplified 3D embedding space. The three dimensions loosely correspond to [royalty, gender, geography].

WordVector [royalty, gender, geography]
1 king [0.9, −0.7, 0.1]
2 man [0.1, −0.8, 0.0]
3 woman [0.1, +0.8, 0.0]
4 result king − man + woman = [0.9−0.1+0.1, −0.7+0.8+0.8, 0.1] = [0.9, 0.9, 0.1]
Nearest neighbor: check cosine similarity to "queen" = [0.9, +0.7, 0.1] cos([0.9,0.9,0.1], [0.9,0.7,0.1]) = (0.81+0.63+0.01) / (√1.63 · √1.31) ≈ 0.97 — nearly identical direction. The analogy works because "man→woman" flips the gender dimension, and the royalty dimension carries through unchanged.

Real World Applications

From word2vec to multimodal AI

Embeddings aren't a research curiosity. They are the load-bearing foundation of modern AI systems. Every major advancement from 2013 onward builds on this idea.

word2vec (2013) — Mikolov et al. at Google proved the analogy arithmetic works at scale. Train a shallow neural network to predict context from a word (skip-gram) or vice versa (CBOW), and the weight matrix becomes a world-class embedding space. The king−queen analogy was the headline result.

GloVe (2014) — Stanford's alternative. Instead of predicting context window by window, GloVe trains on global co-occurrence statistics from the entire corpus at once. Slightly different geometry, similar quality. The key insight: you can derive meaning from co-occurrence counts, not just sequence prediction.

Contextual embeddings: BERT (2018) — word2vec gives every word a single fixed vector. "Bank" in "river bank" and "bank account" get the same embedding. BERT changed this: the embedding for "bank" depends on the entire surrounding sentence. The same word has a different vector in every context. This is why BERT-family models dramatically outperform word2vec on downstream tasks — they capture polysemy.

Sentence transformers (2019–present) — BERT embeddings are per-token. Sentence transformers pool them into a single vector representing the entire sentence's meaning. This enables semantic search at scale: embed a query, find the nearest document embeddings, return the most relevant results. This is now the standard architecture for any retrieval system.

Multimodal embeddings: CLIP (2021) — OpenAI's CLIP trains a text encoder and an image encoder jointly so that "a dog playing fetch" and a photo of a dog playing fetch end up near each other in a shared embedding space. Images and text share coordinates. This enables zero-shot image classification, text-to-image search, and is the backbone of DALL-E and Stable Diffusion.

RAG depends entirely on this concept. Retrieval-Augmented Generation works by embedding your query, searching a vector database for the nearest document chunks, and feeding those chunks to the LLM as context. Without good embeddings, RAG breaks — the wrong documents get retrieved. Every recommender system (Spotify, Netflix, Amazon) uses embeddings: embed users and items into the same space, recommend the nearest items to each user. Semantic search, duplicate detection, clustering, anomaly detection — all are embedding problems at their core.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs about embeddings

These aren't arbitrary mistakes. They reflect reasonable-but-wrong intuitions about what embeddings are. Each one corrects a real confusion.

  • ① Each dimension of an embedding vector has a specific, interpretable meaning.
    Embedding dimensions are not labeled "gender" or "royalty." They are learned by gradient descent and are arbitrary rotations of the latent space. In word2vec, researchers found dimensions that correlated with interpretable features, but this was after the fact. You cannot look at dimension 47 of an embedding and say what it means. The meaning is entirely in the relative geometry across all dimensions together.
  • ② More dimensions always gives better embeddings.
    Bigger is not always better. More dimensions give the model more capacity to represent nuance, but they also require more training data to fill meaningfully, more compute to generate and store, and more memory to serve. OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small (1536 dims) outperforms many larger models. The optimal dimension depends on the dataset size, task, and latency budget — not on "bigger = smarter."
  • ③ Embeddings trained on large data are universal — just pick any good model and use it everywhere.
    Embeddings are tuned to a distribution. A model trained on Wikipedia creates an excellent space for encyclopedia-style text and a poor space for medical clinical notes, code, or tweets. Domain shift degrades retrieval quality significantly. This is why fine-tuned embedding models (legal-BERT, BioBERT, CodeBERT) exist. "Universal" embeddings are a practical convenience, not a free lunch.
  • ④ If two embeddings have high cosine similarity, they represent the same concept.
    High cosine similarity means similar usage patterns, not identical meaning. "Hot dog" (food) and "dog" (animal) may be surprisingly close in some spaces. Antonyms like "good" and "bad" are often near each other because they appear in the same contexts ("that was really _____"). Semantic similarity is a gradient, not a binary, and the threshold for "same concept" depends entirely on your task.
  • ⑤ Cosine similarity is always the right distance metric for embeddings.
    Cosine similarity ignores magnitude, which is fine for normalized embeddings — but many embedding APIs return unnormalized vectors. For those, dot product similarity (which captures both direction and magnitude) can outperform cosine. For some tasks (clustering, anomaly detection), Euclidean distance or Mahalanobis distance is more appropriate. The metric should match the geometric structure the model was trained to produce — always check the model's documentation.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • An embedding is a dense, learned, low-dimensional vector. It replaces one-hot encoding with a representation where similar things are geometrically close.
  • Meaning emerges from training, not design. The model learns geometry as a side effect of optimizing prediction accuracy — semantic structure is never explicitly programmed in.
  • Cosine similarity measures direction, not magnitude. It's the default similarity metric because it captures semantic relatedness independent of text length or frequency.
  • Static embeddings (word2vec, GloVe) give one vector per word. Contextual embeddings (BERT, GPT) give a different vector per word per context — enabling polysemy representation.
  • Embeddings are the foundation of RAG, semantic search, recommender systems, and multimodal AI. Understanding them is not optional for anyone working with modern AI systems.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: You now know what embeddings are and why they work for text. But text is one-dimensional — it has a natural sequence. Images are two-dimensional grids of pixels. There's no obvious order, no natural sequence. How do you learn useful embeddings from 2D spatial data?

That's what Convolutional Neural Networks solve. CNNs are the first architecture that learns embeddings specifically designed for images — representations that capture edges, textures, shapes, and ultimately objects. Now you know what they're building toward.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 What is fundamentally different about an embedding vector compared to a one-hot vector? Give two concrete differences. ▼
Dense vs sparse; semantic vs arbitrary. (1) A one-hot vector has only one non-zero entry out of V dimensions (V = vocab size, e.g. 50,000). An embedding is a dense vector of d dimensions (e.g. 300) where all entries are non-zero floats. (2) In a one-hot space, "cat" and "dog" are exactly as far apart as "cat" and "democracy" — distance is meaningless. In an embedding space, "cat" and "dog" are near each other because they were used in similar contexts during training. The geometry encodes meaning.
02 Why does training a model to predict context (the word2vec objective) produce meaningful geometry as a side effect? ▼
Prediction accuracy requires grouping semantically similar words. Words that appear in similar contexts ("the cat sat," "the dog sat") need to produce similar predictions. The network minimizes prediction error by pushing those words' embeddings to nearby positions — so similar context distributions → nearby vectors. No semantic label is ever given. The structure emerges entirely from the statistical regularities in language: words that behave alike must look alike in embedding space for the model to generalize well.
03 Two documents about the same topic — one 50 words, one 5000 words — are compared using Euclidean distance vs cosine similarity. Which metric would rank them as more similar, and why? ▼
Cosine similarity. The longer document has much larger raw counts, so its embedding vector has a much larger magnitude. Euclidean distance measures the distance between the tips of the vectors — the long document's tip is far from the short one's. Cosine similarity only measures the angle between the vectors. Both documents point in the same direction (same topic) regardless of length, so cosine ≈ 1. For semantic tasks, you care about topic, not length — cosine similarity is correct here.
04 What is the key limitation of word2vec embeddings that BERT's contextual embeddings solve? Give a concrete example. ▼
word2vec assigns one fixed vector per word; BERT assigns different vectors per context. In word2vec, "bank" has one embedding — an average of all its usages. In "river bank" and "bank account" and "I bank with Chase," the same vector is used. BERT processes the entire sentence and produces a different embedding for "bank" in each context, because the surrounding words influence the hidden states via attention. This polysemy representation is why BERT dramatically outperforms word2vec on tasks that require understanding word sense.
05 You have embeddings from Model A and embeddings from Model B. Can you compute cosine similarity between an embedding from A and an embedding from B to measure semantic relatedness? ▼
No — embeddings from different models live in incompatible spaces. Each model finds its own coordinate system through gradient descent. The coordinate axes are arbitrary — there are infinitely many rotations of the space that achieve equally good training loss. Model A might put "cat" at [0.8, 0.2, −0.3] and Model B might put "cat" at [−0.1, 0.9, 0.4]. Cosine similarity between those vectors is meaningless. To compare across models, you need to learn an alignment transformation from one space to the other using paired examples.
06 An embedding lookup is formally identical to a matrix multiply. What is the matrix and what is the vector you're multiplying? ▼
embed(w) = one-hot(w) · E, where E is the embedding matrix of shape (V × d). The one-hot vector selects a row: multiplying a one-hot vector by E is just selecting the i-th row, where i is the word's index. This framing matters because it means the embedding layer is differentiable — gradients can flow through the lookup into the embedding matrix during backpropagation, which is how the embeddings are learned.
07 CLIP trains a text encoder and an image encoder jointly. What training signal teaches them to share a coordinate system? ▼
Contrastive loss on matched text–image pairs. CLIP is trained on 400 million (image, caption) pairs scraped from the web. For each batch, it maximizes the cosine similarity between matched pairs (this photo + its caption) and minimizes it between unmatched pairs (this photo + a different caption). This pushes the image encoder and text encoder to learn a shared coordinate system where semantically aligned text and images converge. No separate alignment step is needed — the shared space emerges from the contrastive objective itself.
08 A RAG system retrieves the wrong documents for a query. You diagnose the problem as poor embedding quality. Name two things you could do to improve retrieval without changing the LLM. ▼
1. Switch to a domain-specific embedding model. If your documents are medical, legal, or technical, a general-purpose embedding model trained on web text may not capture your domain's vocabulary. Fine-tuned models (BioBERT, legal-BERT, or a model fine-tuned on your own data) create better geometry for your domain. 2. Fine-tune the embedding model on your query–document pairs. If you have logs of good and bad retrievals, you can train the embedding model with contrastive loss to bring good query–document pairs closer and push bad ones apart. Both approaches improve the retrieval step without touching the generation LLM.
09 "Good" and "bad" are antonyms but often have high cosine similarity in word embeddings. Why? Does this make the embedding wrong? ▼
No — embeddings encode distributional similarity, not semantic opposition. "Good" and "bad" appear in virtually identical contexts: "that was really ___," "a ___ decision," "the ___ news is." Any model trained on context prediction will push them together because they are distributionally interchangeable. The embedding isn't wrong — it correctly reflects that these words are used similarly. The limitation is that raw cosine similarity cannot distinguish antonymy from synonymy. Tasks requiring that distinction need additional supervision (e.g., sentiment labels, contrastive fine-tuning with positive/negative pairs).
