Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

How language models search their way to accurate answers — beyond the training cutoff.

A language model knows everything up to its training cutoff and nothing after. RAG gives it a live connection to any knowledge base in the world.

The Intuition

The LLM is the reasoner. The vector database is the memory.

A language model is an extraordinary reasoning engine — it understands language, follows instructions, synthesizes ideas, and generates coherent prose. What it cannot do is update its knowledge after training. RAG solves this by separating the job of knowing facts from the job of reasoning about them.

The pipeline is always the same two steps: retrieve, then generate. First, a retrieval system finds the most relevant documents from an external knowledge store — a vector database, a search index, or a document collection. Second, those retrieved documents are injected directly into the LLM's context window alongside the user's query, and the model generates an answer grounded in that retrieved evidence.

  • The LLM contributes language understanding, reasoning, and fluent generation — but not live facts.
  • The vector database stores your documents as dense embeddings and finds semantically similar chunks at query time.
  • The embeddings map text into a high-dimensional space where semantic similarity becomes geometric proximity.
  • The retrieved chunks are prepended to the LLM's prompt — the model reads them like a research assistant reading source notes.

This division of labour means you can update the knowledge base without touching the model. Add new documents, re-embed them, and the system immediately knows the new information. No retraining needed.

Understanding It Deeply

Three questions that force you to think carefully

RAG looks deceptively simple — retrieve, then generate. But each of its components hides design decisions that dominate real-world performance. Before reading the answers, reason through each question yourself.

Think first Why is chunking strategy often more important than the choice of embedding model?

Because an embedding model can only work with what it receives. If your chunks are too large, a single vector must represent too many ideas — the retrieval system fetches a chunk that contains the right sentence buried among irrelevant paragraphs. If chunks are too small, a single concept is split across multiple vectors, and retrieval may return only half of the necessary context. The embedding model's quality is irrelevant if the input chunks are malformed. A mediocre embedding model with well-designed chunks consistently outperforms a state-of-the-art model on poorly-chunked text. Chunk size, overlap, and structure (sentence boundaries, paragraph breaks, headings) are the first-order engineering decisions in any RAG system.

Think first What is the difference between sparse retrieval (BM25) and dense retrieval (embeddings) — and when does each fail?

Sparse retrieval (BM25) uses exact term matching weighted by frequency and inverse document frequency. It finds documents containing the actual words in the query. It is fast, interpretable, and works well when the user knows the right vocabulary. It fails on semantic queries: asking "how do I reduce latency?" will not match a document about "minimising response time" because the words differ.

Dense retrieval (embeddings) maps both query and documents into a shared vector space. Semantic similarity is captured regardless of surface-level word overlap. It handles synonyms, paraphrasing, and cross-lingual queries gracefully. It fails when precise terminology matters — a BM25 search for a product ID, a legal citation, or a specific function name will succeed where dense retrieval drifts toward conceptually similar but wrong documents. Production RAG systems almost always combine both via hybrid retrieval — dense for semantic coverage, sparse for lexical precision — then merge results with rank fusion.

Think first When does RAG fail — and when should you fine-tune instead?

RAG fails in three characteristic ways. First, retrieval fails silently: the relevant document exists but the embedding model ranks it too low, so the model answers from parametric memory (what it learned during training) instead, often confidently and incorrectly. Second, context window saturation: when many chunks are retrieved to hedge against retrieval uncertainty, the model must compress too much context and important details get lost. Third, format and style knowledge: RAG cannot help if the task requires the model to write in a specific tone, follow a particular format, or apply domain-specific reasoning conventions — these are capabilities baked into weights, not content retrievable from a database.

Fine-tuning is the right choice when the model's behaviour needs to change — when you need it to reason differently, adopt a new output format, or internalise knowledge that will never change (like a company's terminology). RAG is right when the knowledge changes over time or the information is too voluminous to fit in training data. Most mature production systems use both: a fine-tuned model for style and reasoning, RAG for current-knowledge grounding.

The Math

Cosine similarity and BM25

Two formulas underpin retrieval. Cosine similarity measures the angle between two embedding vectors — how semantically aligned they are regardless of their magnitude. BM25 scores term-overlap relevance weighted for document length and word rarity.

Cosine Similarity
cos(q, d) = (q · d) / (||q|| · ||d||)
q query vector — the embedding of the user's question d document chunk vector — the embedding of a stored passage q · d dot product — sum of element-wise products ||q|| L2 norm of q — its geometric length in embedding space cos(q,d)ranges from −1 (opposite) to +1 (identical); retrieval keeps top-k values
BM25 Score
BM25(q,d) = Σ IDF(tᵢ) · (f(tᵢ,d) · (k₁+1)) / (f(tᵢ,d) + k₁·(1−b+b·|d|/avgdl))
tᵢ each term in query q IDF(tᵢ)inverse document frequency — rare terms score higher f(tᵢ,d)term frequency — how often tᵢ appears in document d k₁ term frequency saturation constant — typically 1.2–2.0 b length normalisation — typically 0.75; penalises very long documents avgdl average document length in the corpus

Worked example — embedding a query and ranking three chunks by cosine similarity:

StageDetail
1 Embed the query. User asks: "What is cosine similarity?" The embedding model outputs a 4-dim vector (simplified): q = [0.6, 0.8, 0.0, 0.0] . Normalised: ||q|| = √(0.36+0.64) = 1.00
2 Three stored chunks (pre-embedded):
d₁ = [0.5, 0.7, 0.1, 0.0] ||d₁|| ≈ 0.866 — "Cosine similarity measures vector angle..." d₂ = [0.0, 0.1, 0.9, 0.4] ||d₂|| ≈ 0.990 — "Euclidean distance measures straight-line..." d₃ = [0.7, 0.6, 0.0, 0.2] ||d₃|| ≈ 0.943 — "Dot products project one vector onto another..."
3 Compute cosine similarity for each chunk:
cos(q,d₁) = (0.6×0.5 + 0.8×0.7 + 0×0.1 + 0×0) / (1.00 × 0.866) = 0.860 / 0.866 ≈ 0.993 cos(q,d₂) = (0.6×0.0 + 0.8×0.1 + 0×0.9 + 0×0.4) / (1.00 × 0.990) = 0.080 / 0.990 ≈ 0.081 cos(q,d₃) = (0.6×0.7 + 0.8×0.6 + 0×0.0 + 0×0.2) / (1.00 × 0.943) = 0.900 / 0.943 ≈ 0.954
4 Rank by relevance and select top-2 chunks for context:
Rank 1: d₁ (0.993) — "Cosine similarity measures vector angle..." ← most relevant Rank 2: d₃ (0.954) — "Dot products project one vector onto another..." ← second Rank 3: d₂ (0.081) — "Euclidean distance..." ← discarded (below threshold)
d₁ and d₃ are injected into the LLM prompt. d₂ is excluded. The LLM receives: [system prompt] + [chunk d₁] + [chunk d₃] + [user question]. It can only answer from this context — it cannot "remember" d₂ was excluded. The quality of the final answer is bounded by the quality of this retrieval step.

From Concept to Production

Where RAG actually runs in the world

RAG is the architecture behind most production LLM applications that require accurate, current, or proprietary information.

Customer support bots. A company embeds its entire support knowledge base — thousands of articles, policy documents, and product specs. When a user asks a question, the system retrieves the three most relevant passages and the LLM synthesises an answer grounded in official documentation. The model cannot hallucinate policies it can retrieve. When policies change, the knowledge base is re-embedded; the LLM is untouched.

Internal knowledge bases. Enterprises embed internal wikis, Slack conversations, engineering runbooks, and meeting transcripts. Employees query in natural language and get answers attributed to specific internal sources — with citations. This is impossible with a fine-tuned model alone, since internal knowledge changes daily.

Code documentation Q&A. Developer tools like GitHub Copilot Chat embed repository codebases and documentation. A query like "how does the authentication middleware work?" retrieves the relevant source files and docstrings, which the LLM uses to explain the actual code — not an approximation from training data.

The production RAG stack:

1 Chunking — split documents into overlapping passages of 256–512 tokens; preserve sentence and paragraph boundaries; add metadata (source, date, section).
2 Embedding — run each chunk through an encoder (e.g. text-embedding-3-small, E5, BGE) to produce a dense vector; store in a vector database (Pinecone, Weaviate, pgvector, Chroma).
3 Indexing — build an approximate nearest-neighbour index (HNSW, IVF) over all stored embeddings for sub-millisecond retrieval at scale.
4 Retrieval — embed the user query; run ANN search to fetch top-k candidates; optionally fuse with BM25 sparse results via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF).
5 Reranking — pass retrieved candidates through a cross-encoder reranker (e.g. Cohere Rerank, BGE-reranker) which jointly scores query + document at higher accuracy but higher latency. Keep top 3–5.
6 Generation — construct prompt: [system instructions] + [retrieved chunks with source citations] + [conversation history] + [user query]. Send to LLM. The model grounds its answer in the provided context.
A reranker is the step that separates good RAG systems from great ones. Embedding retrieval is optimised for speed (approximate), while a cross-encoder reads the query and each candidate jointly and scores relevance precisely. Adding a reranker typically improves answer quality more than upgrading the embedding model.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs — and why each causes real problems

These misconceptions are connected — each one follows naturally from the previous. Tracing the chain makes them impossible to repeat.

  • ① RAG eliminates hallucination.
    RAG reduces hallucination when retrieval works — but retrieval can fail silently. If the relevant document is absent, embedded poorly, or ranked too low, the model falls back to parametric memory and can still confabulate. Worse, a model with retrieved context can hallucinate citations to the passages it was given, misquoting text that is right in front of it. RAG changes the character of hallucination; it does not eliminate it.
  • ② More context is always better — retrieve as many chunks as possible.
    Context window length is not free. Research consistently shows that LLMs degrade on long contexts — relevant information in the middle of a large context window receives less attention than information at the start or end (the "lost in the middle" phenomenon). Retrieving 20 chunks to be safe often produces worse answers than retrieving 3 high-quality ones. Reranking exists precisely to select a small, high-precision set rather than large, high-recall one.
  • ③ RAG and fine-tuning are alternatives — you pick one.
    They solve different problems and work best together. Fine-tuning adjusts how the model reasons, writes, and formats — its behaviour and style. RAG controls what facts the model has access to at inference time. A fine-tuned model with RAG is better than either alone for most production applications: the model has the right capabilities and the right current knowledge.
  • ④ The embedding model is the most important component — upgrade it first.
    Chunking strategy, reranking, and prompt construction usually dominate. A system with excellent chunking and a reranker using a mid-tier embedding model typically outperforms one with a best-in-class embedding model but naive chunking and no reranking. Benchmark your actual retrieval precision before spending on embedding model upgrades — the bottleneck is often elsewhere.
  • ⑤ Cosine similarity between embeddings directly measures factual relevance.
    Cosine similarity measures geometric proximity in the embedding space, which correlates with semantic similarity as the embedding model defines it. It does not measure relevance to a specific query intent, factual correctness, or answer quality. Two passages about completely different topics can have high cosine similarity if the embedding model encoded their surface features similarly. This is why cross-encoder rerankers — which jointly read query and document — consistently outperform bi-encoder retrieval on relevance judgements.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • RAG decouples knowledge from reasoning: the LLM reasons, the vector database stores and retrieves facts. Update the knowledge base; leave the model untouched.
  • Chunking strategy is often the highest-leverage design decision — badly-chunked text defeats even the best embedding model.
  • Dense retrieval (embeddings) handles semantic queries; sparse retrieval (BM25) handles lexical precision. Production systems use both, merged via Reciprocal Rank Fusion.
  • A cross-encoder reranker that jointly reads query + candidate is a bigger quality upgrade than switching embedding models — it runs on a small candidate set for acceptable latency.
  • RAG reduces hallucination but does not eliminate it; when retrieval fails silently, the model falls back to parametric memory. Retrieval evaluation is as important as generation evaluation.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: RAG gives a model access to a static knowledge base — but what if you need an AI system that can act? One that calls APIs, reads files, executes code, and decides for itself what tools to use next? The architecture that makes this possible — defining a standard protocol for how models connect to external tools and data sources — is what the next chapter covers.

That's the Model Context Protocol (MCP). And it generalises RAG into something far more powerful.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 A RAG system retrieves the correct documents but the LLM still gives a wrong answer. Where should you look first? ▼
Context construction and prompt design. Once retrieval succeeds, the remaining failure modes are: (1) too many chunks are passed in and the relevant one is buried in the middle ("lost in the middle" phenomenon); (2) the prompt does not instruct the model to ground its answer in the provided context; (3) the chunk boundary cuts off critical context, so the model receives a fragment that is semantically close but informationally incomplete. Check chunk quality, trim to top-3 via reranking, and verify the system prompt explicitly references the retrieved context.
02 Query: "What's the refund policy?" gets poor results. A document titled "Money-back guarantee terms" contains the answer but scores low. What retrieval approach would fix this? ▼
Dense retrieval (embedding-based) would close the vocabulary gap. BM25 fails here because the query terms ("refund", "policy") do not appear in the document title or content ("money-back", "guarantee", "terms"). A dense embedding model maps both phrases into nearby regions of the vector space because they are semantically equivalent. Alternatively, hybrid retrieval combining BM25 with dense retrieval via RRF would surface the document through the dense channel even when BM25 misses it.
03 You have two vectors: q = [1, 0] and d = [0.6, 0.8]. What is cos(q, d)? ▼
cos(q, d) = 0.6. q · d = 1×0.6 + 0×0.8 = 0.6. ||q|| = 1.0. ||d|| = √(0.36 + 0.64) = √1.00 = 1.0. cos = 0.6 / (1.0 × 1.0) = 0.6. Note: both vectors happen to be unit vectors, so cosine similarity equals the dot product directly. This is why embedding databases often normalise all vectors at index time — cosine search then reduces to a fast dot-product scan.
04 A document contains the answer exactly but your RAG system never retrieves it. You check — the embedding similarity is 0.62, and your threshold is 0.70. What are two ways to fix this without changing the threshold? ▼
1. Improve chunking. The document may be chunked so that the answer sentence is mixed with unrelated content, diluting the embedding. Re-chunk with smaller windows or by semantic unit so the relevant passage gets its own, focused vector. 2. Query expansion or rewriting. Rephrase the user's query to be more semantically aligned with the document's language, or expand it with synonyms. Some systems use an LLM to generate a hypothetical answer and embed that instead (HyDE — Hypothetical Document Embeddings), which often retrieves better than embedding the raw question.
05 Your support bot needs to always answer in a formal, empathetic tone and never use contractions — regardless of what documents it retrieves. Is this a job for RAG or fine-tuning? ▼
Fine-tuning (or system prompt engineering). Tone, style, and behavioural constraints are model-level properties — they depend on how the model generates text, not on what information it retrieves. RAG affects what facts the model has access to; it has no control over writing style. Fine-tuning on examples of the desired tone, or (more cheaply) a strong system prompt with explicit instructions, is the right lever. RAG still handles the factual grounding; fine-tuning handles the behaviour.
06 What is Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) and why is it used in hybrid RAG retrieval? ▼
RRF is a rank-merging formula that combines result lists from multiple retrievers without needing score normalisation. For each document, its RRF score = Σ 1/(k + rankᵢ) across each retriever list, where k is a constant (typically 60) and rankᵢ is its position in list i. Because it uses only rank positions — not raw scores — it gracefully merges the incomparable score scales of BM25 (unbounded) and cosine similarity (−1 to +1). Documents that appear near the top of both lists get the highest merged scores, capturing both lexical and semantic relevance.
07 A bi-encoder retrieves top-100 candidates in 5ms. A cross-encoder reranks them in 200ms. You need sub-50ms end-to-end latency. What do you do? ▼
Reduce the reranking candidate pool. Cross-encoder latency scales linearly with the number of candidates. Instead of passing all 100 to the reranker, use the bi-encoder to retrieve only the top-10 or top-20 most similar candidates, then rerank that smaller set. This trades a small amount of recall (you might miss a candidate ranked 11–100 that would have been reranked to position 1) for a large latency reduction. In practice, top-20 reranking recovers nearly all the quality of top-100 reranking because the bi-encoder already filters out low-quality candidates.
08 Your product's knowledge base is updated daily. What part of the RAG pipeline must be rebuilt each day — and what can remain static? ▼
Only the changed documents need to be re-embedded and the vector index updated. The embedding model, the LLM, and the retrieval infrastructure (ANN index structure, reranker) are all static. Most production vector databases support incremental updates: delete the old vectors for modified documents, embed the new versions, and upsert them. A full rebuild is only needed when you change the embedding model (because old and new embeddings are incompatible — they live in different spaces). This is why changing embedding models in production is expensive and should not be done lightly.
09 An LLM answers a question correctly without retrieval (from parametric memory). Should you still use RAG for that topic? ▼
Yes — for auditability, currency, and error control. Even when a model's parametric knowledge is currently correct, (1) it will become stale when the underlying facts change; (2) you cannot audit which source the model drew on — RAG enables citations and source attribution; (3) parametric knowledge can be confidently wrong without signalling uncertainty. RAG grounds the answer in a specific, verifiable document that your team controls and can update. The cost is a few hundred milliseconds of retrieval latency — usually worth it for any factual or compliance-sensitive domain.
