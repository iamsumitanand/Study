PCA & Dimensionality Reduction

How to compress a 10,000-pixel face into 50 numbers — and reconstruct it almost perfectly.

A grayscale photo of a human face at 100×100 pixels is a point in 10,000-dimensional space. Every pixel is a coordinate. Yet PCA routinely compresses that face down to 50 numbers — a 200× reduction — while preserving enough structure to reconstruct it, recognise it, and cluster it. Those 50 numbers are the face's fingerprint in variance space. PCA doesn't discard information randomly. It finds the directions in which your data varies most, and keeps only those. Everything else is, mathematically, noise.

The Intuition

Variance is information — shadows reveal structure

Imagine a long, thin cloud of data points stretched diagonally across a 2D plane. If you shine a light from one side and look at the shadow on the floor, you see a wide spread. Shine the light from a different angle and the shadow collapses to almost a single dot. The shadow that preserves the most spread — the most variance — is the most informative projection. That is exactly what PCA computes.

Variance is the mathematical measure of spread. A direction with high variance means the data points are spread out along it — they differ from each other in that direction, so the direction carries discriminating power. A direction with near-zero variance means all points sit at roughly the same value — projecting onto it tells you almost nothing new.

PCA finds a new coordinate system for your data. The first axis (Principal Component 1) points in the direction of maximum variance. The second axis (PC2) points in the direction of maximum remaining variance, subject to being perpendicular to PC1. And so on. You can then represent each data point using only the first few coordinates in this new system — the ones that actually vary — and throw away the rest without losing much.

  • The original features (pixels, sensor readings, gene expressions) form a high-dimensional space.
  • The principal components are new axes that capture the most variance, in order.
  • The projection is the coordinate of each point along those new axes.
  • The explained variance ratio tells you how much information each PC retains.

Understanding It Deeply

Build it from first principles — don't just accept it

PCA is a change of basis. You rotate and rescale the coordinate system so that the axes align with the directions of greatest spread. The key objects are the covariance matrix, which encodes how every pair of features varies together, and its eigenvectors, which point exactly in those directions of greatest spread.

Think first An eigenvector of the covariance matrix is said to represent a "direction of maximum variance." But covariance matrices encode pairwise relationships between features — what does an eigenvector of that actually mean in terms of the original data? Think about what "rotating the axes" does to the cloud of points before reading on.

An eigenvector of the covariance matrix is a linear combination of original features such that, when you project all your data points onto it, the resulting 1D distribution has the highest possible variance. The eigenvalue is that variance. Because the covariance matrix is symmetric and positive semi-definite, all its eigenvectors are orthogonal to each other — they define directions that are statistically uncorrelated in the projected space. Each PC is a recipe for mixing the original features in proportions that maximally spread out the data along one axis, orthogonally to all the others.

Think first PCA works beautifully on a spiral or an elongated ellipse. But what happens if your data lies on a curved manifold — say, points on the surface of a sphere, or a Swiss-roll shape? Before reading on: would PCA recover the underlying 2D structure? Why or why not?

PCA fails for non-linear structure because it can only find linear subspaces. It rotates axes — it cannot curve them. A sphere's surface has two intrinsic dimensions, but any flat 2D projection loses structure: the north pole and south pole collapse toward each other. The covariance matrix of points on a sphere gives nearly equal variance in all three directions, so PCA finds no dominant axes. Non-linear methods like t-SNE, UMAP, or kernel PCA address this by learning curved manifolds, but PCA is blind to them by construction.

Think first You project 1,000 data points from 100 dimensions down to 5 PCs (retaining 80% explained variance). What exactly is "lost"? Is it always bad to lose it? Think about two scenarios: (a) the remaining 20% is random sensor noise; (b) the remaining 20% encodes a rare but meaningful signal.

What is lost is the variance in all directions orthogonal to the 5 chosen PCs — the residual. Each point's projection discards the component of that point that lies outside the chosen subspace; this is called the reconstruction error. In scenario (a), the 20% is noise — discarding it actually improves downstream tasks by denoising. In scenario (b), the rare signal sits in a low-variance direction by definition (few points exhibit it), so PCA happily discards it. This is PCA's fundamental assumption: low variance equals low importance. When that assumption breaks — anomaly detection, rare disease markers, outlier signals — PCA can silently destroy exactly the information you need.

The Math

Covariance, eigendecomposition, explained variance

Three objects do all the work: the covariance matrix encodes pairwise spread, eigendecomposition finds the principal axes, and the explained variance ratio tells you how many dimensions you need.

Covariance Matrix
Σ = (1/n) · Xᵀ X
X mean-centered data matrix, shape (n × d) — each row is a sample, each col a feature n number of data points d number of features (dimensions) Σ d × d symmetric matrix — Σᵢⱼ = covariance between feature i and feature j
Eigendecomposition
Σ = V · Λ · Vᵀ
V matrix whose columns are eigenvectors — the principal components Λ diagonal matrix of eigenvalues λ₁ ≥ λ₂ ≥ … ≥ λ_d — the variance along each PC Vᵀ to project data: Z = X · V_k, where V_k uses only the first k columns
Explained Variance Ratio
EVR_k = λ_k / Σᵢ λᵢ
EVR_k fraction of total variance captured by PC k — pick enough PCs to reach 90–95% λ_k eigenvalue of PC k — equals the variance of the data projected onto that PC Σᵢ λᵢ total variance = sum of all eigenvalues = trace of the covariance matrix

Worked example — 4 points in 2D:

1 Data (already mean-centered): x₁=(2,1) x₂=(−2,−1) x₃=(1,2) x₄=(−1,−2)
2 Compute Σ = (1/4) XᵀX: Σ = [[2.5, 1.5], [1.5, 2.5]]
3 Eigenvalues: det(Σ − λI) = 0 → λ₁ = 4.0, λ₂ = 1.0
4 Eigenvectors (unit): PC1 = [1/√2, 1/√2] ≈ [0.707, 0.707] PC2 = [−1/√2, 1/√2] ≈ [−0.707, 0.707]
EVR: PC1 captures 4/(4+1) = 80% · PC2 captures 1/(4+1) = 20% PC1 points diagonally at 45° — exactly along the elongated axis of the data cloud. Projecting all 4 points onto PC1 alone retains 80% of total variance; you lose the perpendicular spread (PC2) which accounts for 20%.

From Toy to Real

Where PCA actually shows up in the wild

PCA isn't an academic exercise. It appears quietly inside several landmark ML systems — sometimes as the core algorithm, sometimes as a preprocessing step nobody mentions.

Eigenfaces — face recognition

Turk & Pentland (1991) showed that a database of face images, each 10,000 pixels, could be represented by roughly 100 principal components called eigenfaces. Every face in the database is a weighted sum of these eigenface templates. Recognition works by projecting a new face into the eigenface subspace and finding the nearest neighbor. The compression ratio is 100:1 and recognition accuracy stays high — because the discarded variance is mostly noise: lighting flickers, background clutter, JPEG artifacts. The 100 eigenfaces capture the structural variation that actually matters.

Genomics — population structure

A genome-wide association study (GWAS) might measure 500,000 SNPs (genetic variants) per person across 100,000 individuals. Running PCA on this matrix reveals, in its first two principal components, a near-perfect map of geographic ancestry — without any labels. PC1 separates European from East Asian ancestry. PC2 separates North from South within Europe. This works because genetic variation clusters geographically, and PCA finds the directions of maximum variation in the genetic data. Researchers use the first 10–20 PCs as covariates in models to control for population stratification.

Recommender systems — latent factors

A user–movie rating matrix is typically 99% empty — most users haven't rated most movies. Matrix factorization (a close cousin of PCA, using SVD) decomposes this sparse matrix into two low-rank factor matrices: one for users, one for movies. The resulting latent dimensions capture abstract concepts — "action intensity," "romantic tone," "art-house sensibility" — without being given those labels. Netflix's famous $1M Prize was won using this approach. Predicting a missing rating becomes a dot product in a 50-dimensional latent space.

All three applications share the same structure: data lives in a high-dimensional space, but the meaningful variation sits in a much lower-dimensional subspace. PCA finds that subspace. The applications differ only in what "variance" means in context: pixel intensity patterns, allele frequencies, or user preference signals.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs about PCA

These aren't isolated mistakes — they form a chain of confusions that unravels cleanly once you understand what PCA actually optimises.

  • ① PCA selects the most important original features.
    PCA creates entirely new features (principal components) as linear combinations of all original features. PC1 might be 0.7×feature_A + 0.5×feature_B − 0.3×feature_C. No original feature is selected or discarded — all of them participate in every PC. If you want feature selection (choosing which original features to keep), use LASSO or recursive feature elimination instead.
  • ② More variance always means more useful signal.
    Variance and usefulness are not the same thing. A noisy sensor reading or a background illumination artifact can have high variance while carrying zero information about what you care about. Conversely, a rare but critical signal (a tumor marker, a fraud indicator) may sit in a low-variance direction and be exactly what PCA discards. PCA's assumption — high variance = high importance — must be verified for each problem.
  • ③ PCA removes multicollinearity, so it's always good to run it before training.
    PCA does produce uncorrelated components, but blindly applying it before every model is a mistake. It destroys interpretability (you can no longer say "feature X has coefficient Y"), it can discard class-discriminative variance if the low-variance directions happen to separate classes (this is exactly what Linear Discriminant Analysis addresses), and for tree-based models it rarely helps because trees handle correlated features natively. Use PCA deliberately, not as a default preprocessing step.
  • ④ The components explain the "causes" of variation in the data.
    PCA is purely statistical. It finds directions of variance — it says nothing about causation. The first PC of a stock return dataset might correlate with "market beta," but PCA did not discover the market; it found the direction most stocks move together. Labelling PCs with causal interpretations (common in genomics papers: "PC1 = European ancestry") is a post-hoc human interpretation, not something the algorithm established. Interpret with caution — correlation is not causation, and variance direction is not mechanism.
  • ⑤ You need to run PCA on the raw feature matrix.
    PCA must be run on mean-centered and, if features have different scales, standardized data. Without centering, PC1 points toward the mean of the data, not the direction of maximum spread relative to the mean — the covariance matrix is wrong. Without standardization, features measured in large units (e.g. income in thousands) dominate the covariance matrix and PC1 becomes almost entirely that feature. Always center; almost always standardize unless all features share the same units.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • PCA rotates the coordinate system so that axes align with directions of maximum variance. The first k axes capture the most spread — projecting onto them discards the least information.
  • The principal components are eigenvectors of the covariance matrix. The eigenvalue equals the variance along that direction. Eigenvectors are orthogonal — PCs are uncorrelated by construction.
  • Explained variance ratio (λ_k / Σλ) tells you how much information each PC holds. Choose k so the cumulative sum reaches 90–95% for most applications.
  • PCA assumes low variance equals low importance. This breaks for anomaly detection, rare signals, and non-linear manifolds. Check the assumption before trusting the compression.
  • Always mean-center before PCA. Standardize when features have different units. Failure to center makes PC1 point at the mean instead of the spread, corrupting the result.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: PCA reduces dimensions to compress data. But when you use PCA features to train a model, you need to ask: does this compressed representation actually generalise to new data, or did you just overfit to the training variance structure? A model trained on 5 PCs might perform beautifully in-sample and fall apart out-of-sample — not because of PCA, but because of a subtler trade-off between complexity and generalisation.

That tension is the Bias-Variance Tradeoff — and it's the next chapter. It will reframe everything you know about model performance: why simpler models sometimes win, why more data helps, and how to diagnose whether your model is suffering from too much structure or too little.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 You run PCA on a dataset and find that the first two PCs explain 95% of the variance. You drop the remaining PCs. What exactly have you discarded? ▼
The 5% of variance that lies in directions orthogonal to PC1 and PC2. For each data point, you have discarded its component perpendicular to the PC1–PC2 plane — this is the reconstruction error. Whether this loss matters depends entirely on context: if it is sensor noise, discarding it improves your model. If it encodes rare but meaningful signal, you may have just deleted the most important information you had.
02 You compute the covariance matrix of a dataset and find its eigenvalues are all equal. What does this tell you about the data, and how many PCs should you keep? ▼
The data is isotropic — it spreads equally in all directions. All PCs explain the same fraction of variance. There is no preferred direction. You cannot reduce dimensions without losing equal amounts of information in each step. In practice this means PCA offers no compression benefit — you would need to keep all dimensions or accept uniform information loss. This arises with uncorrelated, equal-variance Gaussian noise.
03 Starting from data points (2,1), (−2,−1), (1,2), (−1,−2), which direction is PC1 and what is its eigenvalue? ▼
PC1 = [1/√2, 1/√2] ≈ [0.707, 0.707], eigenvalue = 4.0. The covariance matrix is [[2.5, 1.5], [1.5, 2.5]]. Eigenvalues solve (2.5−λ)² − 1.5² = 0, giving λ₁ = 4, λ₂ = 1. The eigenvector for λ₁ = 4 is [1, 1]/√2 — the 45° diagonal, which aligns with the long axis of the data cloud. EVR: PC1 = 4/5 = 80%, PC2 = 1/5 = 20%.
04 You apply PCA before training a classifier. After projection, your classification accuracy is lower than without PCA. What went wrong? ▼
PCA discarded variance that was class-discriminative. PCA maximises total variance regardless of class labels. The direction that best separates class A from class B may be a low-variance direction overall — perhaps both classes are tightly clustered but in very different places. PCA happily throws that direction away. Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) solves this by finding the direction that maximises between-class variance relative to within-class variance. Use LDA when you have labels and want supervised dimensionality reduction.
05 You forget to mean-center your data before running PCA. How does this corrupt the result? ▼
PC1 points toward the mean of the data instead of the direction of maximum spread. Without centering, the "covariance" matrix is really the second-moment matrix E[XXᵀ], which conflates the mean with the variance. If your data clusters tightly around a point far from the origin, PC1 will mostly encode "the data is over there" rather than "the data spreads in this direction." The principal components become meaningless as measures of variance structure. Centering is non-negotiable; it is baked into the mathematical definition of covariance.
06 A dataset has features measured in meters, kilograms, and seconds. You run PCA without standardizing. What will happen? ▼
PC1 will be dominated by whichever feature has the largest numerical variance. If distances vary from 0 to 10,000 meters while masses vary from 0 to 100 kg, the variance in meters (millions) will dwarf the variance in kg (thousands). PC1 will essentially be a re-scaling of the distance feature, ignoring mass and time entirely. Standardizing (subtracting mean, dividing by std dev) places all features on equal footing before covariance is computed. Skip standardization only when all features are in the same units and comparable magnitudes — e.g. raw pixel intensities in an image dataset.
07 You want to detect fraudulent transactions in a dataset. Will PCA help or hurt your fraud detector? Explain. ▼
PCA is likely to hurt it. Fraud signals are, by definition, rare — they affect a small fraction of transactions. Rare signals occupy low-variance directions in the data because very few points exhibit them. PCA discards low-variance directions first. Your fraud signal may land precisely in the dimensions PCA throws away. Anomaly detection systems (Isolation Forest, autoencoders, one-class SVM) are designed specifically to identify low-density, unusual patterns rather than to compress high-density, common variation. Use PCA for compression and noise reduction, not for anomaly detection.
08 Principal components are always orthogonal to each other. What property of the covariance matrix guarantees this? ▼
The covariance matrix is real and symmetric. The Spectral Theorem guarantees that any real symmetric matrix has a complete set of real, orthogonal eigenvectors. The covariance matrix Σ = (1/n)XᵀX is symmetric by construction (Σᵢⱼ = Σⱼᵢ — the covariance of X with Y equals the covariance of Y with X). Orthogonality of PCs means they are linearly independent and, crucially, that projections onto them are uncorrelated — you cannot get information about PC2 from PC1 alone.
09 t-SNE and UMAP are often used instead of PCA for visualisation. When should you prefer PCA, and when should you use t-SNE or UMAP? ▼
Prefer PCA when: you need a linear, interpretable projection that can be applied to new data, or when you want to preserve global structure and distances. PCA is deterministic, fast, invertible, and can be applied to unseen test points (project using the fitted eigenvectors). Prefer t-SNE or UMAP when: your data has non-linear structure (clusters, manifolds, Swiss rolls) and you primarily care about revealing local neighbourhood structure for visualisation. Caveat: t-SNE cannot transform new data points, distances in the 2D plot are not meaningful globally, and results change with hyperparameters. For preprocessing before a downstream model, use PCA. For exploratory 2D visualisation of cluster structure, use UMAP.
