Autoencoders & VAEs

Compress, represent, generate — and understand what "latent space" really means.

An autoencoder learns to compress an image to 2 numbers, then recreate it — and in those 2 numbers lives a geometry of what the image means. Nearby numbers produce similar images. Interpolate between them and you smoothly morph one concept into another. This is not compression in the JPEG sense. It is a learned map of meaning.

The Intuition

The bottleneck as forced compression

An autoencoder has a simple constraint: pass the input through a narrow bottleneck, then reconstruct it exactly on the other side. To do this well, the network must learn which information is essential and discard the rest.

Think of it as a game of telephone where you are forced to summarise a sentence in two words before passing it on. You cannot keep everything — so you keep what matters most. The bottleneck layer, called the latent space, is those two words. In a well-trained autoencoder, the latent space is not random noise — it is a structured map. Images of the same digit cluster together. Similar digits sit nearby. The geometry of the map mirrors the geometry of meaning.

  • The encoder compresses input → latent vector z.
  • The latent space is the bottleneck — forced low-dimensional representation.
  • The decoder expands z back → reconstructed input.
  • Training signal: minimise reconstruction error — make output match input as closely as possible.

Understanding It Deeply

Three questions to build real intuition

The architecture looks deceptively simple. Two of the most important ideas in modern generative AI — structured latent spaces and learned priors — are buried in the details.

Think first What stops an autoencoder from learning a trivial identity function — just memorising every training example and returning it verbatim, with no actual compression happening?

The bottleneck dimension. If the latent space has fewer dimensions than the input, a perfect memorisation is mathematically impossible — the information simply cannot all fit. The encoder is forced to find a compact code, and the decoder is forced to reconstruct from that code alone. This is the entire mechanism behind the autoencoder's usefulness. Without the bottleneck, you get nothing but a very slow identity function. The bottleneck is not a bug to work around; it is the whole point.

Think first Why is the VAE latent space smooth and continuous, while a plain autoencoder's latent space has "holes" — regions where decoding produces garbage even though you're between two valid codes?

A plain autoencoder learns to place each training example's latent code at exactly one point. Between two codes there is no training signal, so the decoder is never trained on those interpolated regions. Sample a random point in between and you get nonsense. A Variational Autoencoder (VAE) instead encodes each input as a distribution — specifically, a Gaussian with a mean μ and a standard deviation σ. Training samples from that distribution before decoding, so the decoder sees many nearby points and must learn to produce sensible output everywhere in that neighbourhood. The latent space becomes continuous: you can walk between any two valid codes and get meaningful interpolations.

Think first The VAE loss has two terms: reconstruction loss and KL divergence. The reconstruction term says "make the output match the input." What does the KL divergence term actually enforce — what would happen if you removed it?

The KL term measures how far the encoded distribution q(z|x) departs from a standard normal prior p(z) = N(0,I). It penalises the encoder for learning distributions that are too narrow (which would give you point codes, not distributions) or too spread out. Remove the KL term and the encoder collapses back to placing point codes — exactly what a plain autoencoder does. The KL term is what distinguishes a VAE from an AE: it forces every encoded input to occupy a region of latent space with nonzero volume, covering the gaps between training points. This is what makes the VAE a generative model: you can sample directly from N(0,I) and the decoder will produce coherent outputs, because the entire prior has been covered with training signal.

The Math

From reconstruction loss to ELBO

The autoencoder's objective is purely reconstruction. The VAE adds a regularisation term that gives the latent space its generative structure.

AE Reconstruction Loss
L = ||x − x̂||²
x original input x̂ decoder's reconstruction of x from latent code z ||·||² mean squared error — pixel-by-pixel squared difference
VAE Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)
L = E[log p(x|z)] − KL( q(z|x) || p(z) )
E[log p(x|z)] reconstruction term — expected log-likelihood of input under the decoder KL(q||p) regularisation term — how far encoder distribution is from the prior N(0,I) q(z|x) encoder distribution — Gaussian with mean μ and std σ learned from x p(z) prior — standard normal N(0,I); defines the generative latent space

Four-step worked example — AE on a 4-pixel input:

Input x = [0.9, 0.1, 0.8, 0.2], encoder bottleneck = 2D, decoder predicts x̂, learning rate α = 0.1.

1 Encode:  Encoder maps x = [0.9, 0.1, 0.8, 0.2] to latent code z = [0.72, −0.41] (via linear layer + sigmoid). Two numbers capture the "character" of this pixel pattern.
2 Decode:  Decoder maps z = [0.72, −0.41] back to x̂ = [0.85, 0.14, 0.76, 0.25]. Close but not perfect.
3 Reconstruction loss:  L = (0.9−0.85)² + (0.1−0.14)² + (0.8−0.76)² + (0.2−0.25)²
  = 0.0025 + 0.0016 + 0.0016 + 0.0025 = 0.0082
4 VAE KL term:  Encoder also outputs σ = [0.9, 1.1]. Closed-form KL for Gaussian: KL = ½ Σ(μ² + σ² − log σ² − 1)  =  0.128. Total VAE loss: 0.0082 + β · 0.128 where β balances reconstruction vs. regularisation.
Pattern: reconstruction loss drives accuracy; KL term drives smoothness. Increase β and you get a smoother latent space but blurrier reconstructions. Decrease β and you get sharper reconstructions but a latent space with holes. This β-VAE trade-off is central to all modern generative models.

Real Applications

Three places autoencoders and VAEs show up in the wild

The bottleneck architecture is remarkably general. The same principle — compress, structure, reconstruct — appears across image, text, and molecular domains.

Image compression. Learned autoencoders can outperform JPEG on natural images at the same bit rate because they learn what this particular distribution of images looks like, rather than applying a fixed transform. The latent diffusion models behind Stable Diffusion and DALL-E 3 first compress images into a compact latent space using a VAE-like encoder, then run the diffusion process in that compressed space — making generation 64× faster than pixel-space diffusion.

Anomaly detection. An autoencoder trained only on normal data learns to reconstruct normal inputs well. When an anomalous input arrives (a manufacturing defect, a fraudulent transaction, an unexpected server metric), the reconstruction error spikes — the model has never seen this pattern and cannot compress it efficiently. The reconstruction error becomes a real-time anomaly score with no need for labelled anomaly examples.

Drug discovery — molecular generation. A VAE can be trained on a large corpus of known molecules, learning a continuous latent space where nearby points correspond to structurally similar molecules. Researchers can then optimise in latent space — gradient-descend toward high-binding-affinity regions — and decode the resulting latent points into candidate molecules. This turns drug discovery from a discrete combinatorial search into a continuous optimisation problem, a fundamental change in approach.

Common Misconceptions

Five wrong beliefs — and the causal chain connecting them

These five misconceptions form a chain — each one naturally leads to the next. Trace the chain and they become impossible to repeat.

  • ① Autoencoders are just a compression algorithm like ZIP or JPEG.
    ZIP and JPEG use fixed mathematical transforms. Autoencoders learn a compression specific to the data distribution they were trained on — they encode semantic meaning, not just redundancy. Nearby points in the latent space are similar in meaning, not just in pixel values. This is a fundamentally different kind of compression.
  • ② So the latent space is a continuous, smooth map of meaning by default.
    Only for VAEs. A plain autoencoder's latent space has holes: it maps each training point to a location, but the regions between training points are untrained. Sample a random point in between two codes and the decoder has no idea what to produce. The smooth continuous map only emerges when the KL term forces the encoder to use distributions rather than point codes.
  • ③ Then VAEs must reconstruct more accurately than plain autoencoders.
    The opposite — VAEs reconstruct worse at the same bottleneck size. The KL term penalises tight, narrow codes; it forces the encoder to spread probability mass across a region. This deliberately blurs the encoding, which loses precision. You are trading reconstruction sharpness for the ability to generate new samples. It is an explicit loss of fidelity in exchange for a generative capability.
  • ④ The reparameterisation trick is just an implementation detail.
    It is the mathematical core of the VAE. Sampling from a distribution is not differentiable, so gradients cannot flow through it. The reparameterisation trick reformulates the sample as z = μ + σ · ε where ε ~ N(0,I) — making the randomness an external input rather than part of the computation. Without this trick, you cannot backpropagate through the encoder and the VAE cannot be trained at all.
  • ⑤ VAEs are now obsolete — diffusion models are better at generation.
    VAEs are not obsolete — they are embedded inside the best diffusion models. Stable Diffusion uses a VAE to compress images into a compact latent space, then runs the diffusion process there. The VAE handles the pixel-level encoding and decoding; the diffusion model handles the generative distribution in latent space. They are complementary, not competing. Understanding VAEs is understanding the foundation that modern image generators are built on.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • Autoencoders learn to compress via a bottleneck. The constraint forces meaningful representation — the latent code must capture what matters.
  • The AE latent space has holes: only training points are covered. Interpolating between them can produce garbage.
  • VAEs encode each input as a distribution N(μ,σ²) rather than a point. The KL term forces coverage of the prior, filling the gaps.
  • VAE loss = reconstruction term (accuracy) + KL term (smoothness). Increasing β trades reconstruction fidelity for a cleaner latent geometry.
  • Reparameterisation (z = μ + σ·ε) is what makes VAEs trainable — it makes sampling differentiable so gradients can flow through the encoder.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered: VAEs generate by sampling from the prior and decoding. The results are coherent but often blurry — the model hedges its bets, averaging across possibilities. What if instead of learning a smooth continuous space, you set two networks against each other: one generates, the other discriminates? What if the quality signal came not from reconstruction error, but from fooling a critic?

That's Generative Adversarial Networks. And it's the next chapter.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 An autoencoder has a 784-dimensional input (28×28 image) and a 2-dimensional bottleneck. The reconstruction is slightly blurry. What single architectural change would most directly sharpen it — and what would you sacrifice? ▼
Increase the bottleneck dimension. A larger latent space allows more information to survive compression, so reconstruction is sharper. What you sacrifice is the compactness and structure of the latent space — more dimensions means less forced compression, so the latent codes become harder to interpret, interpolate, or use for generation. The blurriness is the autoencoder telling you something real about the information limit of 2 dimensions.
02 You train a plain autoencoder and then try to generate new images by sampling random points from the latent space. Most outputs are garbage. Why — and what would fix it? ▼
The latent space has holes. A plain AE places training examples at specific locations but never trains on the regions in between. Random samples land in untrained regions where the decoder produces nonsense. The fix is a VAE: the KL term forces encoder distributions to overlap with the standard normal prior, meaning the decoder is trained on a continuous region of latent space and random samples from N(0,I) produce coherent outputs.
03 In the VAE loss L = E[log p(x|z)] − KL(q(z|x) || p(z)), you set β = 0 (remove the KL term entirely). What happens to training — and to generation? ▼
Training converges to a plain autoencoder with sharper reconstructions. Generation becomes impossible. Without KL, the encoder learns point codes (σ → 0), which perfectly minimises reconstruction loss. The latent space has no coverage of the prior, so sampling from N(0,I) produces garbage. You can reconstruct training inputs well, but the model has no generative capability at all. This is the β-VAE trade-off: β = 0 gives maximum fidelity, β > 0 gives generative capacity.
04 Why can't you backpropagate through a sampling operation z ~ N(μ, σ²) directly — and what does the reparameterisation trick do about it? ▼
Sampling is a stochastic node — gradients cannot flow through randomness. The sample z depends on the random draw, so there is no gradient of z with respect to μ or σ in the usual sense. The reparameterisation trick separates the randomness: write z = μ + σ · ε where ε ~ N(0,I) is an external random variable, not a function of the parameters. Now z is a deterministic function of μ and σ (with randomness injected from outside), so ∂z/∂μ = 1 and ∂z/∂σ = ε — gradients flow normally. This is what makes the VAE trainable end-to-end.
05 Stable Diffusion uses a VAE. But diffusion models are supposed to be better than VAEs at generation. Why use a VAE at all? ▼
The VAE compresses pixel space into a compact latent space; the diffusion model runs in that latent space. Running diffusion directly on 512×512 pixels requires iterating the denoising process on millions of values per step. Running it in a 64×64 latent space is 64× cheaper. The VAE handles the pixel-level encoding and decoding at high fidelity; the diffusion model handles the generative distribution in the compact latent space. They are not competing — the VAE is infrastructure, the diffusion model is the generative engine running on top of it.
06 An anomaly detection system uses an autoencoder trained on normal server metrics. A new metric pattern arrives and the reconstruction error is 4× higher than average. What does this tell you — and what does it not tell you? ▼
It tells you the pattern is unusual relative to the training distribution. It does not tell you it is an anomaly in the meaningful sense. A high reconstruction error means the encoder cannot efficiently compress this input — it does not appear in the learned structure of normal patterns. But it could also mean the model never saw this type of normal pattern (distribution shift, not an anomaly), or that a new system was added. The reconstruction error is a novelty score, not an anomaly classifier. Ground-truth labelling is still required to set thresholds.
07 You train a VAE on handwritten digits. In the 2D latent space, digits 4 and 9 overlap significantly. You sample from that overlapping region. What do you get — and why? ▼
Images that look like hybrids — recognisably neither a 4 nor a 9, or ambiguously both. The decoder is a function of latent position. In the overlapping region, both digit types contributed training signal during learning, so the decoder learned to produce something in between. This is the VAE's interpolation property in action: the latent space is a map of meaning, and the boundary between two concepts produces something at the semantic midpoint. It is not an error — it is the model expressing genuine uncertainty about what this region represents.
08 Both an autoencoder and PCA reduce dimensionality. What can a deep autoencoder do that PCA fundamentally cannot? ▼
Learn nonlinear structure. PCA finds the directions of maximum variance in the data using a linear projection — it is optimal for linear structure only. A deep autoencoder can learn curved, nonlinear manifolds: it can discover that all images of the digit 3 lie on a curved surface in pixel space, and map that surface to a compact region in latent space. For most real data (images, text, audio), the meaningful structure is highly nonlinear. PCA misses most of it; a deep AE captures it.
09 A VAE trained on molecular structures encodes drug candidates into a 64D latent space. A researcher gradient-descends in that latent space toward higher predicted binding affinity. Why does working in latent space enable this — when working in discrete molecular structure space does not? ▼
Latent space is continuous and differentiable; molecular structure space is discrete and combinatorial. A molecule is a discrete graph of atoms and bonds — you cannot take a gradient with respect to it, and there are 1060 possible drug-like molecules, making exhaustive search impossible. In the VAE's latent space, every point is a real-valued vector, and the binding affinity predictor can be composed with the decoder to give a differentiable function from latent code to predicted affinity. Gradient descent then becomes possible, finding locally optimal latent codes that the decoder translates back to candidate molecules. The VAE converts a combinatorial search into a continuous optimisation problem.
