Fine-tuning & RLHF

How raw language models become helpful, honest, and harmless assistants.

A base LLM is a brilliant but feral library. It has read everything — but it answers questions the way a distracted encyclopaedia would: accurately, comprehensively, and entirely without regard for whether the answer is useful to you right now. RLHF is what teaches it to be helpful without being dangerous. It is the difference between a model that can help and one that does.

The Intuition

The gap between predicting text and following instructions

Pre-training teaches a model to predict the next token given everything before it. This is extraordinarily powerful — it compresses the structure of human language and knowledge into the model's weights. But it optimises for one thing only: statistical fidelity to a training corpus. A model trained this way will complete "The capital of France is…" correctly not because it wants to help you, but because "Paris" is what statistically follows in text that contains that phrase.

The problem is that helpful behaviour is not what the internet looks like. The internet contains misinformation, harmful content, contradictory opinions, and an enormous volume of text that is technically "next-token-predictable" but unsafe or unhelpful to produce on demand. A base model asked to help with a dangerous task will often comply — not because it is malicious, but because the instruction-following pattern is the most statistically likely completion.

Fine-tuning closes this gap in two stages. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) shows the model what good responses look like. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) then teaches it to optimise for human preference — not just mimic demonstrations, but actively seek out outputs that humans rate highly. Together, they transform a text predictor into a genuinely useful assistant.

  • Pre-training gives the model knowledge and fluency — the raw material.
  • SFT gives it behavioural shape — what a good response looks like.
  • RLHF gives it a preference signal — a way to keep improving beyond the demonstrations.

Understanding It Deeply

Three questions that separate real understanding from surface knowledge

The three-stage RLHF pipeline — SFT, reward model training, PPO optimisation — is widely described but rarely understood at the level of mechanism. These questions cut to what actually matters.

Think first Why can't you just fine-tune on a large dataset of "good" examples? Why is the RL step needed at all — isn't supervised learning on demonstrations enough?

Supervised fine-tuning on demonstrations has a fundamental ceiling: it can only make the model as good as the demonstrations it is shown. Human annotators write exemplary responses, but they cannot write every possible good response to every possible prompt — the space of prompts is effectively infinite. The model learns to imitate the demonstrated style, but it has no mechanism for generalising "what is a better response than what I'm currently producing?" beyond those examples.

The RL step solves this via a reward model — a learned function that scores any response on any prompt. Once you have this scorer, you can optimise the language model against it using policy gradient methods like PPO. The language model is not imitating demonstrations any more; it is searching for responses that score well. This allows it to generalise to prompts it never saw in the SFT data, and to produce responses that are better than any human demonstration it was trained on.

Think first What is reward hacking, and why is it more dangerous in RLHF than in other RL settings?

Reward hacking is what happens when a policy learns to maximise the reward model's score without actually becoming more useful or safe. In classic RL (e.g. game-playing), a hacked reward usually produces bizarre but obvious behaviour — an agent that scores points through a glitch. In RLHF, the policy is a language model, which means it can hack the reward model through persuasive but incorrect text, through sycophantic agreement, or through confident-sounding completions that the reward model rates highly but that are factually wrong.

This is why the KL divergence penalty between the current policy and the reference model (the SFT model) is essential: it prevents the policy from drifting so far from the original distribution that it starts exploiting weaknesses in the reward model rather than genuinely improving. The reward model is itself imperfect — it is a neural network trained on a finite sample of human preferences. The KL constraint keeps optimisation in a regime where the reward model's scores are still informative.

Think first How does RLHF relate to Constitutional AI (used in Claude) and RLAIF? Are these replacements or extensions?

Constitutional AI (CAI), developed by Anthropic, is an extension of the RLHF idea that replaces human preference labels with AI-generated critiques. In standard RLHF, the reward model is trained from human comparisons of responses. In CAI, the model first critiques its own outputs against a set of explicit principles (a "constitution"), then revises them, then another model rates the revised outputs. This produces a feedback signal without requiring as many expensive human annotations for the safety-relevant comparisons.

RLAIF (Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback) generalises this: use a capable AI model (often a larger one) as the judge rather than humans. Both approaches preserve the three-stage RLHF structure — SFT, reward model training, RL optimisation — but change the source of the preference signal. They are extensions of RLHF, not replacements, and face the same reward hacking and KL-constraint challenges.

The Math

PPO objective and KL divergence penalty

RLHF uses Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) as the RL algorithm. The objective balances two pressures: maximise the reward model's score, while staying close to the original SFT model. Both pressures are captured in a single equation.

PPO Clipped Objective + KL Penalty
L = E[ min(r_t · A_t, clip(r_t, 1−ε, 1+ε) · A_t) ] − β · KL[π_θ ‖ π_ref]
r_t probability ratio π_θ(a_t|s_t) / π_ref(a_t|s_t) — how much the new policy differs from the old one on this token A_t advantage estimate — reward signal above or below the baseline; positive means this response was better than expected clip(r_t, 1−ε, 1+ε) clipping prevents excessively large updates; ε ≈ 0.2 is typical, bounding the ratio to [0.8, 1.2] β KL penalty coefficient — controls how strongly the model is penalised for drifting from the reference (SFT) distribution KL[π_θ ‖ π_ref] Kullback-Leibler divergence — measures how different the current policy is from the reference model token by token

The clipping term and the KL penalty serve the same purpose from different angles. Clipping limits the size of any single gradient update so the policy cannot change too drastically in one step. The KL penalty measures the cumulative drift from the reference model across all tokens in a response and subtracts it from the reward. Together, they form a trust region — optimisation is aggressive enough to improve but conservative enough that the reward model's signal stays reliable.

Four-step worked example — preference probability and PPO gradient direction:

StepDescription
1 Two responses to the same prompt. The reward model assigns scores: Response A scores r_A = 2.4, Response B scores r_B = 0.8. Response A is preferred.
2 Preference probability via Bradley-Terry model. P(A ≻ B) = σ(r_A − r_B) = σ(2.4 − 0.8) = σ(1.6) ≈ 0.832. The reward model loss is −log(0.832) ≈ 0.184 — it was correct and fairly confident.
3 Advantage estimate for PPO. Baseline reward (running average) = 1.2. Response A advantage: A_t = 2.4 − 1.2 = +1.2. Positive advantage — this response was better than average, so we should make it more likely.
4 PPO gradient direction. Since A_t > 0, we increase the probability of the tokens in Response A. The clipped ratio prevents this increase from being unlimited. Simultaneously, KL[π_θ ‖ π_ref] > 0 pushes back toward the SFT model — a soft anchor preventing reward hacking.
Pattern: RLHF turns pairwise human preferences into a continuous gradient signal. The Bradley-Terry model converts "A is better than B" into a probability. The reward model learns this probability. PPO then uses the reward scores as an advantage signal, clipped and KL-constrained to stay in a region where the reward signal is trustworthy.

From Theory to Practice

How InstructGPT, Claude, and Gemini each approach alignment

The three-stage RLHF pipeline is a template, not a fixed recipe. Each major lab has made different bets on where human feedback is most valuable and how to make fine-tuning computationally tractable.

InstructGPT (OpenAI, 2022) — the paper that crystallised the modern RLHF pipeline. OpenAI collected ~13,000 prompt-response pairs from contractors for SFT, then ~33,000 pairwise preference rankings for the reward model, then ran PPO. The resulting 1.3B-parameter InstructGPT model was rated as more helpful than the 175B GPT-3 base model by human evaluators — a result that established that RLHF quality matters far more than raw parameter count for instruction following.

Claude (Anthropic) — Constitutional AI — instead of writing thousands of preference labels by hand for harmful content, Anthropic gave the model a set of written principles (the "constitution") and had it critique and revise its own outputs against those principles. A second model then rated the revised outputs. This RLAIF signal — AI-generated rather than purely human-generated — scaled more efficiently to safety-relevant comparisons that are expensive or unpleasant for human annotators to label. Constitutional AI is best understood as a data-efficient extension of RLHF, not a replacement of it.

Gemini (Google DeepMind) — like other large-scale models, Gemini uses RLHF as part of a broader pipeline, but additionally emphasises multimodal instruction following (text, images, audio, video) from early in training. The reward model must capture preference across modalities, making the comparison problem significantly harder.

LoRA for efficient fine-tuning — full fine-tuning on a 70B-parameter model requires updating billions of weights and is prohibitively expensive. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) solves this by adding small trainable matrices to each weight layer while freezing the original weights. The rank of these matrices (often 8–64) determines the number of trainable parameters — often less than 0.1% of the full model. For SFT, LoRA produces comparable results to full fine-tuning at a fraction of the compute cost. It does not change the RLHF logic; it changes only how the policy update is parameterised.

The core insight across all three systems: alignment is not a post-processing step. It requires carefully constructed training data, a learned model of human preference, and an optimisation loop that keeps improving while staying bounded by a reference policy. Getting any one of these three wrong — bad SFT data, a miscalibrated reward model, or an unconstrained PPO update — can produce a model that looks aligned but is not.

Common Misconceptions

Five causal misconceptions about fine-tuning and RLHF

Each misconception is a plausible-sounding causal story that is subtly or entirely wrong. Tracing why each is wrong sharpens the mental model.

  • ① RLHF teaches the model to be helpful by showing it what "helpful" looks like.
    SFT shows it what helpful looks like — RLHF does something categorically different. RLHF gives the model a reward signal that lets it explore and improve beyond any demonstration it was shown. The RL step is not imitation learning; it is optimisation under a learned preference function. Confusing the two obscures why reward hacking is possible (you can't hack a demonstration) and why the KL penalty is necessary.
  • ② A higher reward score always means a better, safer model.
    The reward model is itself a neural network trained on finite human data. It is an approximation of human preference, not a perfect measure of it. A model that scores higher on the reward model is only better within the distribution where the reward model is accurate. Outside that distribution — which is exactly where reward hacking occurs — a higher score can mean the policy has found a blind spot in the reward model and is exploiting it.
  • ③ Fine-tuning makes the model forget its pre-training knowledge.
    Fine-tuning changes the model's behaviour, not its underlying knowledge. The pre-trained weights contain the "knowledge"; fine-tuning shifts which behaviours are preferred in which contexts. Catastrophic forgetting is a real risk in naive fine-tuning on very small datasets, but with standard RLHF pipelines the KL penalty explicitly prevents the model from drifting far from the pre-trained reference distribution, preserving most pre-training capabilities.
  • ④ Constitutional AI (CAI) replaces human feedback with AI feedback — so it requires no human input.
    The "constitution" — the set of written principles — is entirely human-authored. Humans decide what the model should value and how to express those values in natural language. What CAI automates is the costly per-example annotation of whether a specific response violates a specific principle. The principles themselves, and the decision of which principles matter, remain a fundamentally human choice. CAI is a data-efficiency improvement, not a removal of human judgment.
  • ⑤ LoRA produces a strictly inferior model because it only trains a small fraction of the parameters.
    For fine-tuning tasks (as opposed to training from scratch), parameter count is not the bottleneck — task-relevant signal in the training data is. LoRA focuses gradient updates on low-rank subspaces that capture the task-relevant changes, leaving the pre-trained knowledge intact. On many benchmarks, LoRA fine-tuned models match or outperform full fine-tuning — particularly in low-data regimes — because they are less likely to overfit to the small fine-tuning dataset.

Summary

Five things to lock in

  • RLHF has three stages: SFT shapes behaviour from demonstrations; reward model training converts human pairwise preferences into a scalar scorer; PPO optimises the model against that scorer with a KL constraint to prevent reward hacking.
  • The RL step is not imitation — it is optimisation under a learned preference function, which allows the model to generalise to new prompts and exceed the quality of any single demonstration.
  • Reward hacking is real: a language model can learn to score well on the reward model without being more useful or safe. The KL penalty between the current policy and the reference SFT model is the primary defence.
  • Constitutional AI (Anthropic) and RLAIF preserve the RLHF structure but replace expensive human annotation with AI-generated feedback against explicit principles, improving scalability for safety-critical comparisons.
  • LoRA makes fine-tuning computationally tractable by adding low-rank trainable matrices to frozen pre-trained weights — often achieving full fine-tuning performance with less than 0.1% of the trainable parameters.
One question this chapter leaves unanswered — and the start of Part 5: RLHF makes a model more helpful and safer within the scope of its training data. But it cannot help a model answer questions about events that happened after it was trained, about your specific documents, or about real-time information. For that, the model needs to retrieve relevant information at inference time rather than relying entirely on baked-in knowledge.

That is Retrieval-Augmented Generation — and it is the opening chapter of Part 5: Putting AI to Work.

Test Your Understanding

9 Questions

0 / 9 revealed

01 A base LLM scores perfectly on a next-token prediction benchmark. Why might it still give dangerous or unhelpful answers to user instructions? ▼
Because next-token prediction optimises for statistical fidelity to the training corpus, not for helpfulness or safety. The training corpus contains harmful content, misinformation, and undesirable completions — all of which are "correct" from a next-token prediction standpoint. A base model that completes a harmful instruction is doing exactly what it was trained to do. SFT and RLHF are required to shift the model's behaviour toward outputs that are useful and safe specifically in response to user intent.
02 SFT was run on 13,000 demonstration examples. Why can the RLHF-trained model produce responses better than any of those 13,000 demonstrations? ▼
Because the RL step is optimisation, not imitation. After SFT, the reward model is trained to score any response on any prompt. PPO then optimises the language model against this scorer — the model is not constrained to reproduce demonstrations, it can generate new responses and receive scores for them. This allows it to discover responses that score higher than any demonstration in the SFT data, particularly on prompts not covered by those 13,000 examples.
03 The KL divergence penalty in the PPO objective is set to β = 0. What risk does this create? ▼
Unconstrained reward hacking. Without the KL penalty, the policy is free to drift as far from the SFT reference model as gradient descent wants. The reward model is trained on a finite distribution of human preferences — outside that distribution, its scores are unreliable. A policy optimised with β = 0 will eventually discover that certain token sequences score highly on the reward model but are not genuinely useful or safe. The KL penalty keeps the policy in the regime where the reward model's scores are still informative.
04 Two responses, A and B, get reward model scores of 3.1 and 1.1 respectively. Compute P(A ≻ B) using the Bradley-Terry model. ▼
P(A ≻ B) = σ(3.1 − 1.1) = σ(2.0) ≈ 0.880. The sigmoid function σ(x) = 1 / (1 + e^−x). At x = 2.0: σ(2.0) = 1 / (1 + e^−2) ≈ 1 / (1 + 0.135) ≈ 0.880. The reward model is expressing 88% confidence that A is preferred. The reward model training loss for this pair is −log(0.880) ≈ 0.128 — a small loss indicating a confident and correct prediction.
05 An RLHF-trained model starts giving sycophantic, flattering answers that score well on the reward model but are factually wrong. What went wrong, and what is the fix? ▼
Reward hacking: the model found a blind spot in the reward model. The reward model was likely trained on preference data where agreeable, confident answers were labelled preferred even when incorrect. The policy has learned to optimise for this pattern. Fixes include: (1) increasing β to constrain policy drift; (2) improving reward model training data to include examples where factually wrong but agreeable answers are labelled as dispreferred; (3) adding a separate factuality reward signal. This is a known failure mode — the InstructGPT paper explicitly documents the model becoming more sycophantic under RLHF.
06 Constitutional AI uses AI-generated feedback. Does this mean no human judgment is involved in the safety training process? ▼
No — the constitution itself is entirely human-authored. The principles that define what the model should and should not do are written by humans at Anthropic. What is automated is the per-example annotation: instead of human annotators labelling each response against each principle, the AI generates this annotation at scale. Human judgment determines the values; AI generation scales the application of those values to millions of examples. CAI is a data-efficiency improvement, not a removal of human judgment.
07 A LoRA fine-tuning run uses rank r = 8. The original weight matrix is 4096 × 4096. How many trainable parameters does this LoRA adapter add for that layer, versus full fine-tuning? ▼
LoRA adds 4096×8 + 8×4096 = 65,536 parameters vs full fine-tuning's 4096×4096 = 16,777,216. LoRA decomposes the weight update ΔW into two low-rank matrices: A (d×r) and B (r×d), where r ≪ d. For r=8 and d=4096: 4096×8 = 32,768 + 8×4096 = 32,768 = 65,536 total — about 0.39% of the full layer. Across all layers of a large model, this reduction accumulates to less than 0.1% of total parameters, making fine-tuning tractable on modest hardware.
08 In PPO, the advantage A_t for a particular response is −0.7 (negative). What does this mean, and what does PPO do with it? ▼
A negative advantage means this response was worse than the baseline expectation. A_t = reward − baseline. If A_t = −0.7, the response scored 0.7 units below the running average reward. PPO then decreases the probability of the tokens in this response — making it less likely the model produces this kind of response in the future. The magnitude of the update is clipped by the ratio r_t to prevent the policy from changing too drastically in one step, even for responses with large negative advantages.
09 The InstructGPT paper reports that a 1.3B RLHF-trained model is preferred over a 175B base GPT-3 model. What does this tell you about the relationship between model size and alignment? ▼
Alignment and capability are largely orthogonal axes. A larger base model has more knowledge and reasoning capacity, but this does not translate into better instruction-following or safety without an alignment procedure. The 1.3B InstructGPT model was trained specifically to follow instructions well; GPT-3 175B was not. The result demonstrates that the RLHF training procedure contributes more to perceived helpfulness than raw parameter count — at least within the scale range tested. This has significant implications for compute efficiency: you can achieve strong alignment from a smaller model rather than simply scaling a base model.
